## Abstract

The Relation-First Organization Programme already proves that relation is primitive but stable relation is triadic. In *Triadic Closure and Recursive Residual Theory*, the primitive role set is:

\[
\mathcal P=\{\mathsf R,\mathsf O,\mathsf Z\},
\]

with:

\[
\mathsf R=\text{relation / mediation},
\]

\[
\mathsf O=\text{observer-chart / local visibility},
\]

\[
\mathsf Z=\text{zero-boundary / initial-terminal discipline}.
\]

The same paper already proves that all six proper subfamilies of \(\mathcal P\) are closure-incomplete: three singleton configurations and three dyadic configurations. It also supplies the triadic mediation inequality:

\[
\Delta(X,Z)\preceq \Delta(X,Y)\otimes\Delta(Y,Z),
\]

whose metric realization is the ordinary triangle inequality.

This paper does not introduce a new primitive theory. It extracts the coupling calculus already implicit in the existing stack.

The central claim is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{relation-first}
\Rightarrow
\text{triadic declaration}
\Rightarrow
\text{recursive triadic substitution}
\Rightarrow
\text{closure-constrained coupling}.
}
\]

The main new organizational object is the **triadic closure atlas**: a charted network of declared triads and admissible role-interfaces in which every local coupling is closure-complete, every substituted role is declared, every residual is recorded, and no path step is left dangling.

This paper also identifies the **Minimal Non-Stranded Coupling Cell** as a target theorem for the closure-atlas programme: under residual-recursive and no-stranding assumptions, a local triadic coupling cell is expected to require at least three triads unless an explicit internal-recursive substitute supplies the missing continuation interface. This statement is treated here as a target theorem unless a full proof is supplied in the relevant section.

## 1.6 Triadic recursion modes

The previous section separates recursion pressure from realized recursion. There is a further structural point.

The three recursion modes already used by the programme align with the three primitive triadic roles:

\[
\mathcal P=\{\mathsf R,\mathsf O,\mathsf Z\}.
\]

They are not unrelated kinds of recursion. They are face-dominant modes of recursive relation.

### 1.6.1 \(\mathsf Z\)-recursion: residual recursion

Residual recursion is boundary-dominant recursion.

It arises when proper nonterminal closure leaves a residual:

\[
\Omega\neq0.
\]

The residual is a boundary phenomenon: the closure has not collapsed to the lower boundary and has not saturated at the upper boundary, but it also has not vanished relative to the declared local task.

Thus:

\[
\boxed{
\text{residual recursion is }\mathsf Z\text{-dominant recursion.}
}
\]

It is the recursion of unresolved boundary.

### 1.6.2 \(\mathsf R\)-recursion: trace recursion

Trace recursion is mediation-dominant recursion.

It arises through admitted path traces:

\[
T_0\to T_1\to T_2
\]

or through admitted branch/update paths in Recursive Chart Learning.

The dominant structure is transition, mediation, path, and continuation.

Thus:

\[
\boxed{
\text{trace recursion is }\mathsf R\text{-dominant recursion.}
}
\]

It is the recursion of the path.

### 1.6.3 \(\mathsf O\)-recursion: substitution recursion

Substitution recursion is observer/readout-dominant recursion.

It arises when a role that was being used as a face becomes visible as nontrivial and must itself be opened into a triad:

\[
\mathsf Z
\Rightarrow
(\mathsf Z_Z,\mathsf R_Z,\mathsf O_Z),
\]

or similarly for \(\mathsf R\) and \(\mathsf O\).

The dominant structure is role-visibility: what was functioning as an unexpanded role is re-read as structured enough to require its own declaration.

Thus:

\[
\boxed{
\text{substitution recursion is }\mathsf O\text{-dominant recursion.}
}
\]

It is the recursion of role-visibility.

## Theorem 1.6.4 — Triadic Recursion Decomposition

**Presentation note.** This theorem uses the Relation-to-Triad Declaration Theorem stated in §2. It is placed here because the recursion decomposition is part of the existing-theorem-stack review; the logical dependency is explicit.

If relation is primitive and stable declared relation is triadic, then any admitted recursion of a relation-context has three primitive face-modes:

\[
\mathsf Z\text{-recursion}
\]

through residual / boundary remainder;

\[
\mathsf R\text{-recursion}
\]

through trace / mediation / path update;

\[
\mathsf O\text{-recursion}
\]

through substitution / charted role-visibility.

Any admissible recursive step must either instantiate all three faces or declare how the missing faces are supplied by an existing closure context.

### Proof

By Relation-to-Triad Declaration, a declared relation requires boundary, mediation, and visibility roles. A recursive step is itself a relation claim: it claims that a prior closure, trace, or role-opening conditions a later structure. Therefore the recursive step must itself be triadically declared.

The boundary face appears as residual or nonterminal closure condition; this is \(\mathsf Z\)-recursion. The mediation face appears as trace, path, update, or transition; this is \(\mathsf R\)-recursion. The visibility face appears as role-recognition, charted substitution, evaluator, or readout; this is \(\mathsf O\)-recursion.

If any face is missing and not supplied by an existing closure context, the recursion claim is underdeclared. \(\square\)

# 2. Relation-to-triad declaration

## Definition 2.1 — Declared relation role structure

A relation \(R\) is **declared for inference** only when it supplies, or is supplied with, role data:

\[
T_R=(\mathsf R_R,\mathsf O_R,\mathsf Z_R).
\]

Where:

- \(\mathsf R_R\) is the mediation / relation mode;
- \(\mathsf O_R\) is the observer-chart / visibility / readout mode;
- \(\mathsf Z_R\) is the zero-boundary / admissibility / distinction mode.

## Theorem 2.2 — Relation-to-Triad Declaration Theorem

If relation is primitive and a relation is declared for inference, then the relation must instantiate mediation, visibility, and boundary roles.

### Proof

A relation used in inference must say:

1. what mediates or connects;
2. what distinction or boundary makes the relation determinate;
3. how the relation is visible, charted, registered, evaluated, or admitted.

If any of these is absent, then the relation cannot be distinguished from non-relation, identity, arbitrary perspective, or invisible arrow. Such a relation is underdeclared and violates No Undeclared Relation. Therefore declared relation requires the triadic role structure. \(\square\)

## Corollary 2.3 — NUR is triadically interpreted

No Undeclared Relation means:

\[
\boxed{
\text{no relation whose mediation, boundary, or visibility face is undeclared.}
}
\]

Thus No Undeclared Boundary and No Undeclared Observer are not rival principles. They are face-corollaries of NUR.

A role subfamily is any subset:

\[
Q\subseteq\mathcal P.

A role subfamily is **proper** if:

\[

## Theorem 3.2 — Six Instability Configurations

There are exactly six proper nonempty subfamilies of the primitive role set \(\mathcal P=\{\mathsf R,\mathsf O,\mathsf Z\}\), and each is closure-incomplete.

\[

By Triadic Closure, the full role set is the first closure-complete structure relative to the boundary--mediation--visibility requirements. Therefore every proper nonempty subfamily is closure-incomplete. \(\square\)

# 4. Triadic substitution

## 4.1 Motivation

A role may be instantiated by a substitute.

For example:

\[
\mathsf Z
\]

may appear as:

- zero-boundary;
- threshold;
- support;
- tolerance;
- endpoint;
- local/global cut.

\[
\mathsf R
\]

may appear as:

- bridge;
- transition;
- gluing map;
- quotient;
- transport;
- update rule;
- cocycle.

\[
\mathsf O
\]

may appear as:

- chart;
- observer;
- readout;
- evaluator;
- calibrator;
- measurement;
- empirical semantics.

A substitute is not automatically valid. If it does nontrivial relation work, it must itself be declared.

## Definition 4.2 — Role substitute

A **role substitute** for role \(\mathsf X\in\mathcal P\) is any structure \(S_{\mathsf X}\) used to perform that role in a local relation.

## Definition 4.3 — Nontrivial role substitute

A role substitute is **nontrivial** when its own validity depends on internal distinctions, transitions, readouts, thresholds, or preservation conditions.

## Theorem 4.4 — Triadic Substitution Principle

A nontrivial substitute for any primitive role is admissible only if it either satisfies the role primitively in the local task or carries a declared triadic closure sufficient for that role.

### Proof

Let \(S_{\mathsf X}\) be a nontrivial substitute for role \(\mathsf X\). Since it is nontrivial, its use depends on relation-like internal structure: distinctions, transitions, readouts, thresholds, or preservation. By relation-first, that internal structure must itself be declared. By Theorem 2.2, declared relation requires mediation, visibility, and boundary roles. Therefore \(S_{\mathsf X}\) must either be primitive for the local task or carry a declared triadic closure sufficient for its role. \(\square\)

## Corollary 4.5 — Residual-recursive role expansion

A declared triad may expand recursively when one of its role substitutes is nontrivial and leaves a proper residual.

The expansion has two stages:

1. **recursion pressure**: a proper nonterminal residual indicates that the local closure is not exhausted;
2. **realized recursion**: an admitted update or trace uses that residual to change later available structure.

Thus recursive triadic substitution is not arbitrary self-similarity. It is the \(\mathsf O\)-dominant recursion mode: role-visibility makes a previously unexpanded face visible as relation-capable. Once opened, the substitution still requires residual pressure and admitted trace/update to be part of a complete recursive triadic closure.

The native terms are:

\[
\boxed{
\text{residual-recursive triadic substitution}
}
\]

when closure residuals drive the expansion, and:

\[
\boxed{
\text{trace-recursive triadic substitution}
}
\]

when admitted traces update later available role structures.

# 5. Coupling between triads

## Definition 5.1 — Declared triad

A declared triad is:

\[
T=(\mathsf R_T,\mathsf O_T,\mathsf Z_T)
\]

with mediation, visibility, and boundary roles declared for a local relation.

## Definition 5.2 — Triadic coupling

A **coupling** between triads \(T_A\) and \(T_B\) is a declared relation:

\[
T_A\to T_B.
\]

Since it is a relation, it must itself have a triadic declaration:

\[
T_{A\to B}
=
(\mathsf R_{A\to B},\mathsf O_{A\to B},\mathsf Z_{A\to B}).
\]

Thus the real form is:

\[
\boxed{
T_A
-
T_{A\to B}
-
T_B.
}
\]

This is the triadic generalization of source--bridge--target.

## Theorem 5.3 — Coupling Requires Triadic Closure

A coupling between declared triads is admissible only if the coupling relation is itself triadically declared.

### Proof

A coupling \(T_A\to T_B\) is a relation claim. By No Undeclared Relation and Theorem 2.2, any declared relation must instantiate mediation, visibility, and boundary roles. Therefore the coupling must have a declared triadic role structure. \(\square\)

## Corollary 5.4 — No bare triad-to-triad arrow

A bare arrow:

\[
T_A\to T_B
\]

is underdeclared unless the coupling triad \(T_{A\to B}\) is supplied.

# 6. Role interfaces and no dangling role

## Definition 6.1 — Role interface

A **role interface** occurs when a role-face or role-substitute from one triad is used in another triadic structure.

Example:

\[
\mathsf O_A
\]

from \(T_A\) is used as the observer/readout face of a coupling involving \(T_B\).

## Definition 6.2 — Landed role interface

A role interface is **landed** when the imported role-face completes a declared role requirement in a closure-complete triadic context.

## Definition 6.3 — Dangling role interface

A role interface is **dangling** when a role-face is inserted into another structure but does not land in a closure-complete triadic role context.

## Proposition 6.4 — No Dangling Role Interface

A role-face inserted into another triadic structure is admissible only if it lands in a declared closure-complete context.

### Proof

A dangling role interface is a relation attempt that has not landed in an admissible triadic role context. By the no-dangling path principle in Recursive Chart Learning, a transition attempt that does not land in another admissible chart is not a trace-admitted path step. Applying the same relation-first discipline to role interfaces, a non-landed role-face cannot serve as an admissible coupling step. Therefore a role-face must land in a closure-complete context. \(\square\)

## Corollary 6.5 — No hanging face is not a new no-go

The "no hanging face" idea is a projection of already existing results:

1. no dangling path trace step;
2. closure-incomplete role subfamily no-go;
3. no dyadic transfer without bridge;
4. endpoint equality does not recover path equality.

Thus it should not be introduced as an independent master principle.

# 7. Triadic closure atlas

## Definition 7.1 — Triadic closure atlas

A **triadic closure atlas** is a collection:

\[
\mathfrak T
=
(\{T_i\},\{C_{ij}\},\Omega,\operatorname{Adm},\operatorname{Update})
\]

where:

1. each \(T_i\) is a declared triad;
2. each \(C_{ij}\) is an admissible triadic coupling between \(T_i\) and \(T_j\);
3. \(\Omega\) records closure residuals;
4. \(\operatorname{Adm}\) admits or rejects coupling steps;
5. \(\operatorname{Update}\) updates later available couplings or charts.

## Definition 7.2 — Closure-complete interface

An interface \(C_{ij}\) is closure-complete when its coupling relation supplies mediation, visibility, and boundary roles.

## Definition 7.3 — Admissible atlas path

An atlas path is a sequence:

\[
T_{i_0}\to T_{i_1}\to\cdots\to T_{i_n}
\]

whose coupling steps are all landed and closure-complete.

## Theorem 7.4 — Closure Atlas Admissibility Theorem

A triadic closure atlas supports admissible pathing only through landed, closure-complete coupling interfaces.

### Proof

Each path step is a coupling relation. By Theorem 5.3, each coupling requires triadic declaration. By Proposition 6.4, each role interface must land in a closure-complete context. Therefore an atlas path is admissible only if each step is landed and closure-complete. \(\square\)

## Corollary 7.5 — Bridge selection is atlas navigation

Recursive Bridge Calibration can be read as navigation through a triadic closure atlas.

Candidate bridge selection becomes:

1. identify active triad;
2. locate missing role or unstable subconfiguration;
3. identify candidate coupling triad;
4. test closure-completeness of interface;
5. admit, reject, or record residual;
6. update available paths.

This is a refinement of RBC, not a replacement.

# 8. Triadic face-recovery and visibility bridges

This section integrations the general structural content first exposed in the photon case study.

The photon case made visible a general pattern:

\[
\frac{E}{p}
=
\frac{\omega}{k}
=
c.
\]

This is not merely a photon-specific formula. It is an instance of a more general valued-triad pattern: when a triad has a declared compatibility law and valid residuals or quotients, a face may be recovered from the other two.

The mathematical setting is not new. Residuated lattices, quantales, and Lawvere-style generalized metrics already study operations with residuals, adjoints, and generalized composition. The programme's contribution is the triadic placement: identifying which residual recovers which face of a boundary--mediation--visibility triad.

---

## 8.1 Definition — Valued triad

A **valued triad** is a declared triad:

\[
T=(\mathsf Z,\mathsf R,\mathsf O)
\]

whose faces take values in declared value domains and whose compatibility is governed by a declared operation or relation.

The value domains may be numerical, ordered, algebraic, categorical, logical, or task-relative.

---

## 8.2 Definition — Triadic compatibility operator

A **triadic compatibility operator** is a declared relation:

\[
\kappa(\mathsf Z,\mathsf R,\mathsf O)=0
\]

or, in a multiplicative / compositional presentation:

\[
\mathsf O=\mathsf Z\otimes\mathsf R.
\]

The operator states how the three faces cohere.

The operation \(\otimes\) is not assumed to be ordinary multiplication. It may be addition, composition, tensor, monoidal product, action, transition, comparison, or another declared operation.

---

## 8.3 Definition — Face-recovery operation

A **face-recovery operation** is a declared residual, quotient, adjoint, or comparison operator that recovers one face from the other two.

For example:

\[
\mathsf Z=\mathsf O\oslash_Z\mathsf R,
\]

\[
\mathsf R=\mathsf Z\oslash_R\mathsf O,
\]

\[
\mathsf O=\mathsf Z\otimes\mathsf R.
\]

Not every triad admits such operations. Face-recovery requires a declared algebraic or order-theoretic setting.

---

## Theorem 8.4 — Triadic Face-Recovery Compatibility

Let:

\[
T=(\mathsf Z,\mathsf R,\mathsf O)
\]

be a declared valued triad with compatibility law:

\[
\mathsf O=\mathsf Z\otimes\mathsf R.
\]

If the relevant residual or quotient operations are declared and valid, then any one face may be recovered from the other two:

\[
\mathsf Z=\mathsf O\oslash_Z\mathsf R,
\]

\[
\mathsf R=\mathsf Z\oslash_R\mathsf O,
\]

\[
\mathsf O=\mathsf Z\otimes\mathsf R.
\]

### Proof

The readout recovery is the declared compatibility law. If \(\oslash_Z\) is a declared residual solving for \(\mathsf Z\), then applying it to \((\mathsf O,\mathsf R)\) recovers the boundary face. If \(\oslash_R\) is a declared residual solving for \(\mathsf R\), then applying it to \((\mathsf Z,\mathsf O)\) recovers the mediation face. Each recovery is valid only relative to the declared operation and domain. \(\square\)

---

## 8.5 Remark — Mathematical setting

Theorem 8.4 is not a new theorem of residuation theory.

Its algebraic home is the family of residuated structures, including residuated lattices and quantales, and the enriched-categorical setting behind Lawvere generalized metrics.

The programme's contribution is the role interpretation:

\[
\boxed{
\text{residuation can be read as triadic face-recovery.}
}
\]

This lets a bridge audit ask which face is being solved for, which two faces are being held fixed, and whether the residual operation is declared.

---

## 8.6 Definition — Visibility bridge

A **visibility bridge** is a declared coupling:

\[
A
-
\mathfrak V
-
\mathcal O
\]

where:

- \(A\) is a source organization;
- \(\mathfrak V\) is a mediation/readout relation-packet;
- \(\mathcal O\) is an observer/readout chart.

The bridge makes selected distinctions of \(A\) visible in \(\mathcal O\).

---

## Theorem 8.7 — Visibility Bridge Objecthood

Let \(A\) be a source organization and let \(\mathcal O\) be an observer/readout chart. If a declared visibility bridge:

\[
A-\mathfrak V-\mathcal O
\]

supplies boundary, mediation, and readout data, and if the resulting readout is stable across the relevant admissible chart class, then \(A\)'s visible charted objecthood is locally recovered in \(\mathcal O\).

### Proof

Charted Objecthood requires stable chart-visible organization across an admissible chart class. The source organization \(A\) alone does not supply its visibility in \(\mathcal O\). The visibility bridge supplies a declared relation from \(A\) to \(\mathcal O\): it bounds the relevant distinctions, mediates their transmission or registration, and supplies the readout. If that readout is stable across the declared chart class, then the objecthood condition is locally satisfied. \(\square\)

# 9. Local upper triads and typed micro-triad atlases

This section integrations the general machinery first exposed in the dimension-recovery case.

The point is not to add a new primitive. It is to make explicit how an already-declared triad can contain typed subordinate triads.

---

## 9.1 Definition — Local upper triad

A **local upper triad** for a task \(\mathcal T\) is a closure-complete triad:

\[
\mathfrak C_{\mathcal T}
=
(\mathsf Z_{\mathcal T},\mathsf R_{\mathcal T},\mathsf O_{\mathcal T})
\]

such that every admissible claim for the task is either:

1. a completion of \(\mathfrak C_{\mathcal T}\);
2. a face-recursion inside one of its faces;
3. a typed micro-triad inside it;
4. or a residual recorded against it.

"Upper" means local-to-task closure. It does not mean globally maximal, metaphysically prior, or terminal.

---

## 9.2 Definition — Typed micro-triad

Let:

\[
\mathsf Z_{\mathcal T}=(z_1,\ldots,z_m),
\quad
\mathsf R_{\mathcal T}=(r_1,\ldots,r_n),
\quad
\mathsf O_{\mathcal T}=(o_1,\ldots,o_k).
\]

A **typed micro-triad** is a component triple:

\[
(z_i,r_j,o_\ell)
\]

that forms an admissible local relation-context inside \(\mathfrak C_{\mathcal T}\).

It is admissible only if:

1. \(z_i\) bounds or declares the domain/tolerance/regime of \(r_j\);
2. \(r_j\) mediates a relation whose distinction can be read by \(o_\ell\);
3. \(o_\ell\) is a legitimate readout for the \(z_i,r_j\) pair;
4. any compatibility / quotient / residual / recovery operation is declared.

Thus:

\[
\boxed{
\text{component selection is not enough;}
}
\]

\[
\boxed{
\text{typed compatibility is required.}
}
\]

---

## Theorem 9.3 — Typed Micro-Triad Admissibility

A component triple selected from:

\[
\mathsf Z_{\mathcal T}\times\mathsf R_{\mathcal T}\times\mathsf O_{\mathcal T}
\]

is an admissible micro-triad only if its boundary component bounds the mediation component and its readout component reads a distinction produced by that mediation.

### Proof

By Relation-to-Triad Declaration, a relation claim requires boundary, mediation, and readout. Selecting one component from each face does not by itself establish a relation claim. If the boundary does not bound the mediation, or if the readout does not read the mediated distinction, the triple is underdeclared. Therefore typed compatibility is required. \(\square\)

The atlas is not a brute-force enumeration of all component triples. It is a typed closure structure.

# 10. Minimal coupling cells and non-stranding

## 10.1 Relation-triad locus convention

Every triad in this programme is a **relation triad**.

A triad may occupy a source role, target role, coupling role, observer role, boundary role, or update role in a local configuration, but it is not an object-first unit. Calling some of them "object triads" risks reintroducing the object-first parser.

The correct vocabulary is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{relation-triad locus}.
}
\]

A **relation-triad locus** is any declared triadic role structure:

\[
T=(\mathsf R_T,\mathsf O_T,\mathsf Z_T)
\]

that occupies a place in a closure atlas.

Thus source, coupling, target, return, update, and residual-carrying positions are all relation-triad loci.

---

## Definition 10.2 — Minimal transfer cell

A **minimal transfer cell** from relation-triad locus \(T_A\) to relation-triad locus \(T_B\) is a local triadic configuration containing enough declared data for an admissible relation from \(T_A\) to \(T_B\).

By No Undeclared Relation and Theorem 5.3, the transfer has form:

\[
T_A
-
T_{A\to B}
-
T_B.
\]

The middle locus \(T_{A\to B}\) is also a relation triad: the coupling relation itself.

---

## Theorem 10.3 — Minimal Transfer Cell Theorem

An admissible transfer from one relation-triad locus to another requires at least three relation-triad loci: source, coupling, and target.

### Proof

An admissible transfer from \(T_A\) to \(T_B\) is a relation claim. A bare arrow:

\[
T_A\to T_B
\]

is dyadic and underdeclared. By Theorem 5.3, the coupling relation must itself be triadically declared. Therefore the minimal expanded cell is:

\[
T_A
-
T_{A\to B}
-
T_B.
\]

So at least three relation-triad loci are required. \(\square\)

---

## Corollary 10.4 — The user's minimum-two observation, transfer form

Relative to an active triad \(T_A\), a minimal admissible transfer cell contains at least two other relation-triad loci:

\[
T_{A\to B}
\quad\text{and}\quad
T_B.
\]

This confirms the minimum-two observation at the level of transfer.

---

## Corollary 10.5 — Two directly attached triads are underdeclared

A pair:

\[
T_A,T_B
\]

with no declared coupling triad does not form an admissible transfer cell.

### Proof

The pair asserts a relation between \(T_A\) and \(T_B\) without declaring the mediation/coupling relation. This is dyadic transfer and violates No Undeclared Relation. \(\square\)

---

## Definition 10.6 — Recursive coupling cell

A **recursive coupling cell** around a relation-triad locus \(T_0\) is a local sub-atlas containing:

1. \(T_0\);
2. at least one landed coupling from \(T_0\) to another relation-triad locus;
3. a proper residual or nonterminal closure condition that generates recursion pressure;
4. an admitted update, return, or onward path that realizes that pressure by changing later available couplings involving \(T_0\), its successor, or the coupling itself;
5. closure-complete interfaces at all coupling sites.

This imports the programme's existing recursion discipline: finite sequence alone is not recursion; residual pressure must be realized by admitted trace/update. In the language of Section 1.6, a recursive coupling cell must close the \(\mathsf Z\)-face of residual, the \(\mathsf R\)-face of trace/update, and the \(\mathsf O\)-face of readout/substitution.

---

## Definition 10.7 — Stranded coupling cell

A coupling cell is **stranded** when it lands in a terminal endpoint with no declared residual, update, return, or onward admissible path while still claiming recursive continuation.

This is the role-interface version of the no-dangling path-trace discipline.

---

## Definition 10.8 — Internal-recursive substitute

An **internal-recursive substitute** is a declared role, coupling triad, target triad, or update structure whose internal residual/update mechanism supplies the continuation that would otherwise require another external coupling step.

It has two components:

1. a proper nonterminal residual or local closure gap;
2. a declared update/admission rule that uses that residual to open a return, recalibration, or onward path.

For example, a coupling triad \(T_{0\to1}\) may carry an update rule that returns to \(T_0\) or opens a later admissible path. If that residual/update mechanism is declared, the local cell can be non-stranded without adding a new external target.

---

## Proposition 10.9 — Transfer-completeness does not imply recursion-completeness

A minimal transfer cell:

\[
T_0
-
T_{0\to1}
-
T_1
\]

may be transfer-complete but recursion-incomplete.

### Proof

By Theorem 8.3, the cell is sufficient for admissible transfer if its coupling triad is declared and landed. But recursive completeness requires both a proper residual/nonterminal closure condition and an admitted update or trace-induced change to later available structure. The transfer cell does not guarantee either component. Therefore transfer-completeness does not imply recursion-completeness. \(\square\)

---

## Theorem 10.10 — Minimal Non-Stranded Recursive Cell

A minimal transfer cell:

\[
T_0
-
T_{0\to1}
-
T_1
\]

is non-stranded as a recursive cell only if \(T_{0\to1}\), \(T_1\), or another declared interface supplies:

1. residual recursion pressure;
2. an admitted return, update, recalibration, or onward path that realizes that pressure;
3. a readout/substitution role by which the residual and update are registered as changing the later available structure.

### Proof

A recursive coupling cell requires more than transfer. By Definition 8.6, it requires a residual or nonterminal closure condition, an admitted update/trace path that changes later available structure, and a readout/substitution role by which that change is registered. If neither the coupling triad nor the target triad nor any declared interface supplies these, the local path lands at \(T_1\) and terminates. If it still claims recursion, it is stranded by Definition 8.7. Therefore non-stranded recursion requires triadic closure of residual pressure, trace/update, and readout/substitution. \(\square\)

---

## Theorem 10.11 — Residual Recursion Realization Theorem

Let \(T\) be a proper nonterminal triadic closure in a coupling atlas. If \(T\) carries a proper residual \(\Omega_T\), then \(T\) generates recursion pressure. If an admitted update rule:

\[
U_\Omega(\Omega_T,T)
\]

is declared, then \(T\) becomes residual-recursive relative to that update.

### Proof

A proper nonterminal triadic closure is neither lower-boundary collapse nor terminal totalization. Its unclosed remainder is the residual \(\Omega_T\). That residual is precisely the data available for a further closure attempt. This gives recursion pressure.

If an admitted update rule \(U_\Omega\) is declared, then the residual changes the later available structure by definition of the update. If a readout/substitution role registers that changed availability, the recursion is triadically declared: residual supplies the \(\mathsf Z\)-face, update/trace supplies the \(\mathsf R\)-face, and readout/substitution supplies the \(\mathsf O\)-face. That is realized recursion in the sense of Recursive Chart Learning. \(\square\)

---

## Theorem 10.12 — External continuation expands the cell

If no internal-recursive substitute is supplied in:

\[
T_0
-
T_{0\to1}
-
T_1,
\]

and recursive continuation must proceed to a distinct later relation-triad locus \(T_2\), then the expanded declared path contains at least five relation-triad loci:

\[
T_0
-
T_{0\to1}
-
T_1
-
T_{1\to2}
-
T_2.
\]

### Proof

The path from \(T_0\) to \(T_1\) requires coupling triad \(T_{0\to1}\). The onward path from \(T_1\) to \(T_2\) is another relation claim and therefore requires coupling triad \(T_{1\to2}\). Thus the expanded path contains:

\[
T_0,\quad T_{0\to1},\quad T_1,\quad T_{1\to2},\quad T_2.
\]

These are five relation-triad loci. \(\square\)

---

## Corollary 10.13 — Minimum-two is necessary but not always sufficient for recursion

Relative to \(T_0\), a minimal transfer cell contains two other relation-triad loci:

\[
T_{0\to1},\quad T_1.
\]

This is sufficient for transfer.

It is sufficient for non-stranded recursion only when one of those loci carries a declared internal-recursive substitute that closes residual, trace/update, and readout/substitution faces. Otherwise external continuation expands the cell.

---

## 10.14 Programme-native lesson

The safe principle is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{no stranded recursive coupling.}
}
\]

There is no maximum-degree constraint. A triad may participate in many couplings. The constraint is local closure and non-stranding at every interface.

The user's minimum-two insight is correct for admissible transfer under the relation-triad convention. For recursion, it becomes a conditional lower bound: the two additional loci must also supply residual pressure and admitted continuation, or the path must expand.

The fully programme-native statement is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{proper nonterminal closure supplies recursion pressure;}
}
\]

\[
\boxed{
\text{declared residual/update admission realizes recursion.}
}
\]

# 11. Topology and combinatorics of closure atlases

## 11.1 Native object before representation

The native object is not first a graph.

It is a **triadic closure atlas**.

Graph, hypergraph, categorical, or simplicial representations are useful only after the programme-native role/coupling/closure conditions are declared.

The native data are:

1. relation-triad loci;
2. coupling triads;
3. role substitutions;
4. instability labels;
5. residuals;
6. admitted traces;
7. closure-bound updates.

---

## 11.2 Simplicial representation of a single triad

A closure-complete triad:

\[
(\mathsf Z,\mathsf R,\mathsf O)
\]

can be represented combinatorially as a filled 2-simplex:

- vertices: singleton faces \(\mathsf Z,\mathsf R,\mathsf O\);
- edges: dyadic faces \((\mathsf Z,\mathsf R)\), \((\mathsf R,\mathsf O)\), \((\mathsf Z,\mathsf O)\);
- filled triangle: closure-complete triad.

The six unstable configurations are exactly the nonempty proper faces of this simplex:

\[
\boxed{
3\text{ vertices}+3\text{ edges}=6\text{ unstable configurations}.
}
\]

The filled 2-simplex is not the primitive. It is a representation of the already-declared triadic closure requirement.

### Proposition 11.2.1 — Face representation of instability

The six closure-incomplete role configurations are in bijection with the nonempty proper faces of the 2-simplex representing \((\mathsf Z,\mathsf R,\mathsf O)\).

### Proof

A 2-simplex has three vertices and three edges. These correspond respectively to the singleton and dyadic proper nonempty subfamilies of \(\{\mathsf Z,\mathsf R,\mathsf O\}\). By Theorem 3.2, those six subfamilies are exactly the closure-incomplete configurations. \(\square\)

---

## 11.3 Coupling as an incidence path

A minimal transfer cell:

\[
T_A
-
T_{A\to B}
-
T_B
\]

can be represented as an incidence path through relation-triad loci.

Here:

- \(T_A\) is the source role locus;
- \(T_{A\to B}\) is the coupling role locus;
- \(T_B\) is the target role locus.

A direct edge:

\[
T_A\to T_B
\]

without \(T_{A\to B}\) is an underdeclared dyadic representation of a triadic coupling.

---

## 11.4 Substitution as local simplex expansion

When a role-face becomes nontrivial, substitution unfolds it into a new triad.

For example:

\[
\mathsf Z
\Rightarrow
(\mathsf Z_Z,\mathsf R_Z,\mathsf O_Z).
\]

Combinatorially, this is like replacing a vertex by a new filled triangle, together with compatibility interfaces back to the parent triad.

This is not free expansion. The substituted triad must land in the parent closure context and cannot leave a dangling role interface.

---

## 11.5 Closure atlas incidence graph

A triadic closure atlas admits an incidence graph representation:

\[
G_{\mathfrak T}=(V_T,V_C,E),
\]

where:

- \(V_T\) are relation-triad loci;
- \(V_C\) are coupling triads;
- edges in \(E\) connect coupling triads to the relation-triad loci they mediate.

A path in this graph is admissible only when each coupling triad is closure-complete and each interface is landed.

This graph is not the theory. It is a bookkeeping representation of the closure atlas.

### Proposition 11.5.1 — Incidence representation of closure-atlas paths

Every finite triadic closure atlas admits a bipartite incidence representation whose vertex classes are relation-triad loci and coupling triads. An atlas path is admissible only along incidence paths whose coupling vertices are closure-complete and whose role interfaces are landed.

### Proof

Construct the incidence graph with one vertex for each relation-triad locus and one vertex for each coupling triad. Add an edge whenever a coupling triad mediates a relation-triad locus. By definition of admissible atlas path, each path step must pass through a declared coupling triad and each interface must be landed. Thus admissible atlas paths correspond only to incidence paths satisfying those conditions. \(\square\)

---

## 11.6 Residual topology

A closure atlas may contain:

- open paths;
- stranded endpoints;
- closure cycles;
- residual cycles;
- holonomy-like obstruction loops;
- connected closure components;
- local-to-global gluing obstructions.

A cycle is not automatically valid. It must carry declared boundary, mediation, and readout roles at every local coupling.

A loop with residual:

\[
\Omega\neq0
\]

is a possible recursion-pressure site.

---

## 11.7 Combinatorial obstruction labels

Each local failure can be labelled by its missing or unstable role:

\[
o_{\mathsf Z},\quad o_{\mathsf R},\quad o_{\mathsf O},
\]

or by a higher interface failure:

\[
o_{\rm sub},\quad o_{\rm coup},\quad o_{\rm res},\quad o_{\rm trace},\quad o_{\rm recurread},\quad o_{\rm strand},\quad o_{\rm over}.
\]

This gives Recursive Bridge Calibration a combinatorial pathing rule: repair the first unstable role-interface whose repair unlocks the greatest downstream closure.

---

## 11.8 Caution

The programme should not import graph, hypergraph, category, or simplicial language as primitives.

The correct order is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{declared triadic closure atlas first; mathematical representation second.}
}

# 16. Summary

The programme already contains the core of a triadic coupling calculus.

The native structure is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{relation-first}
\Rightarrow
\text{triadic declaration}
\Rightarrow
\text{proper closure residual}
\Rightarrow
\text{recursion pressure}
\Rightarrow
\text{triadic recursion decomposition}
\Rightarrow
\text{closure-constrained coupling}
\Rightarrow
\text{triadic closure atlas}.
}
\]

Valued triads may also admit declared compatibility operators and face-recovery operations. In such cases, any face may be solved from the other two when the algebra supports it.

Local upper triads and typed micro-triad atlases describe how a closure-complete task triad can contain admissible subordinate triads without treating all component triples as meaningful. This is the architectural home for the machinery later applied in the dimension-recovery paper.

The three recursion modes are:

\[
\boxed{
\mathsf Z\text{-recursion}
=
\text{residual recursion}
}
\]

\[
\boxed{
\mathsf R\text{-recursion}
=
\text{trace recursion}
}
\]

\[
\boxed{
\mathsf O\text{-recursion}
=
\text{substitution recursion}.
}
\]

The next architectural update should revise:

1. Triadic Closure;
2. No Undeclared Boundary;
3. No Undeclared Observer;
4. No Undeclared Relation;
5. Recursive Bridge Calibration.

The key operational result is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{do not test bridges randomly;}
}
\]

instead:

\[
\boxed{
\text{navigate the closure atlas by finding and repairing unstable role interfaces.}
}
\]

This is the programme-native charting and pathing rule for articulated triads. The worked examples show that the rule is mechanically checkable: identify missing role faces, repair the first unstable interface, and update the closure-bound record.
