## Abstract

This paper defines Recursive Bridge Calibration, a relation-first method for selecting and revising bridge candidates under declared source, target, boundary, readout, calibrator, obstruction, and trace-update data.

The method is designed for claims of the form

\[
X\Rightarrow Y,
\]

where the source structure, target distinctions, and mediating bridge are not yet adequately separated. Recursive Bridge Calibration expands such claims into source-bridge-target form, identifies the target distinctions at stake, consults the theorem/no-go register, generates candidate bridge paths, calibrates those candidates against explicit success and failure conditions, and records the resulting trace state.

The procedure is recursive because success, partial success, and failure all update the later branch field. A failed bridge attempt is therefore not discarded; it becomes a traceable obstruction that constrains the next attempt.

The paper's main worked orientation is the Lorentzian bridge chain, but the method is not a Lorentzian-specific construction. Its public role is methodological: it formalizes how the programme chooses, tests, repairs, and sequences bridges without treating analogy, formal resemblance, or partial success as verified transfer.

# 1. Adjacent methodological literatures

Recursive Bridge Calibration is programme-native, but it is not isolated from existing philosophy-of-science methodology.

It is adjacent to several established traditions.

## 1.1 Analogical reasoning

The procedure is close to disciplined analogical reasoning: a source structure is proposed as a bridge template for a target structure, and the analogy is admitted only after the relation, target distinction, and preservation condition are declared.

The difference is that Recursive Bridge Calibration does not treat analogy as sufficient. Analogy generates a candidate bridge; calibration decides whether it is admissible.

## 1.2 Research programmes and progressive repair

The method also resembles the Lakatosian distinction between progressive and degenerative research programmes. A bridge repair is progressive when it reduces obstruction, recovers a declared target distinction, or turns a vague failure into a precise next obligation. It becomes degenerative when it only protects an overbroad claim by adding undeclared rescue conditions.

The calibrator boundedness theorem is the programme-native version of this discipline: too little declaration is \(z_-\); totalizing rescue is \(z_+\); admissible work lies between.

## 1.3 Robustness and negative results

The method treats failure as information. A failed bridge localizes what is missing: relation, boundary, readout, chart compatibility, tolerance, physical interpretation, or empirical semantics.

This is aligned with robustness and negative-results methodology: failures are not discarded but used to constrain the next trace.

## 1.4 Operational reconstruction

The procedure is also close to operational reconstruction. It asks what roles must be declared for a target distinction to be recovered: event algebra, measurement/readout, admissibility rule, update rule, covariance, or calibration.

The programme's distinctive contribution is to put these roles into a general relation-first bridge procedure.

## 1.5 Scientific realism, structural realism, and pragmatism

Recursive Bridge Calibration is best read as a structural-realist method with pragmatist safeguards.

It is structural-realist because it treats recovery of declared relations and preserved distinctions as the unit of unification. This is close in spirit to the ontic structural-realist orientation defended by Ladyman and Ross.

It is pragmatist in the limited sense that it does not claim the procedure guarantees truth. The procedure can terminate in success, stable partial success, or explicit blockage. Physical truth and empirical realization require further calibration and testing. This caution is compatible with van Fraassen-style empiricist restraint about what formal success licenses.

Thus the method does not replace realism with usefulness. It makes realism inspectable by forcing the relation, boundary, readout, and calibrator into declared form.

# 2. Motivation

The programme repeatedly faces claims of the form:

\[
X\Rightarrow Y.
\]

Examples:

\[
\text{positive information geometry}\Rightarrow\text{Lorentzian structure},
\]

\[
\text{causal order + number}\Rightarrow\text{geometry},
\]

\[
\text{scale readout}\Rightarrow\text{dynamics},
\]

\[
\text{formal action-like evaluator}\Rightarrow\text{physical field equation}.
\]

Relation-first discipline says that each compressed arrow must be expanded:

\[
\boxed{
X-\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}-Y.
}
\]

But once the bridge form is explicit, a new question appears:

\[
\boxed{
\text{which bridge should be attempted first?}
}
\]

Recursive Bridge Calibration answers that question by using the programme's theorem and no-go registers to determine which bridge is upstream, which bridge is blocked, which bridge is premature, and which bridge unlocks the most downstream structure with the least overclaim.

# 3. Primitive schema

The method is built from the programme's primitive triadic structure:

\[
\boxed{
\text{boundary}
-
\text{relation / mediation}
-
\text{observer / readout}.
}
\]

Every bridge candidate must declare all three faces.

## 3.1 Relation face

The relation face answers:

\[
\boxed{
\text{what connects source to target?}
}
\]

Examples:

- order embedding;
- count-volume calibration;
- residual interpretation;
- action calibration;
- chart transition;
- evaluator-induced update.

## 3.2 Boundary face

The boundary face answers:

\[
\boxed{
\text{what distinction separates success, failure, inside, outside, admissible, or limiting?}
}
\]

Examples:

- \(\lambda\) in path-polarity;
- \(\Lambda\) in bounded-action dynamics;
- \(\varepsilon_V\) in count-volume calibration;
- null/boundary class in order-cone structure;
- no-go threshold for bridge adequacy.

## 3.3 Observer/readout face

The observer/readout face answers:

\[
\boxed{
\text{what makes the target distinction visible, comparable, or evaluable?}
}
\]

Examples:

- \(N_{\rm pp}\) as interval-count readout;
- \(V_{\rm pp}\) as scaled readout;
- \(\mathcal A_{\rm pp}\) as history evaluator;
- \(\operatorname{DynAdm}\) as admissibility readout;
- chart covariance as atlas-level comparability.

Thus a bridge is never merely an arrow. It is a structured relation with boundary and readout.

# 4. Core definitions

## Definition 4.1 — Target distinction family

A **target distinction family** is a declared set:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq D_Y
\]

of distinctions claimed to be recovered in the target context.

Examples:

\[
D_{\rm order},\quad
D_{\rm scale},\quad
D_{\rm dyn},\quad
D_{\rm phys}.
\]

A target claim is underdeclared until its target distinction family is named.

---

## Definition 4.2 — Bridge candidate

A **bridge candidate** from source \(X\) to target \(Y\) is a tuple:

\[
\mathfrak B_i
=
(R_i,B_i,O_i,P_i)
\]

where:

1. \(R_i\) is the declared relation;
2. \(B_i\) is the boundary/admissibility condition;
3. \(O_i\) is the observer/readout/evaluator;
4. \(P_i\) is the preservation condition.

A bridge candidate is meaningful only relative to a target distinction family \(D_Y'\).

---

## Definition 4.3 — Bridge adequacy

A bridge candidate \(\mathfrak B_i\) is **adequate for \(D_Y'\)** if:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_i}(D_X).
\]

That is, the target distinctions are recoverable from the source distinctions under the declared bridge.

---

## Definition 4.4 — No-go register

The **no-go register** is the set:

\[
\mathcal N
\]

of known programme obstructions.

Typical entries include:

- No Undeclared Relation;
- No-Free-Transfer;
- positive geometry is not Lorentzian signature;
- causal order is not scale;
- counting is not continuum volume;
- metric/scale is not dynamics;
- action-like evaluator is not physical action;
- admissible-history dynamics is not field equations;
- chart covariance is not physical covariance;
- formal residual is not physical residual;
- no total preservation under proper transfer.

The register may grow.

---

## Definition 3.5 — Theorem register

The **theorem register** is the set:

\[
\mathcal T
\]

of positive bridge patterns already proved or conditionally established.

Typical entries include:

- path-polarity bridge recovers path-polarity;
- path-polarity plus composition closure yields preorder;
- antisymmetry yields partial order;
- local finiteness gives finite interval counting;
- scale unit gives scaled discrete readout;
- evaluator/admissibility gives admissible-history dynamics;
- chart covariance gives atlas-level comparability;
- persistence margin gives trace stability;
- bridge factorization gives component obligations.

The theorem register supplies constructive templates.

---

## Definition 3.6 — Bridge trace / closure-bound record

The **bridge trace / closure-bound record** is the current record:

\[
\mathcal L
=
(\mathcal S,\mathcal F,\mathcal P,\mathcal O)
\]

where:

- \(\mathcal S\) records successful bridges;
- \(\mathcal F\) records failed bridges;
- \(\mathcal P\) records partial successes;
- \(\mathcal O\) records open bridge obligations.

This trace record is the programme-scale analogue of a Recursive Chart Learning trace record.

---

## Definition 3.7 — Candidate branch field

Given a source context, target family, theorem register, no-go register, and trace record, the **candidate branch field** is:

\[
\mathcal B(T;\mathcal T,\mathcal N,\mathcal L)
=
\{\mathfrak B_1,\ldots,\mathfrak B_n\}.
\]

It is the set of bridge candidates available for forward calibration.

# 5. Recursive Bridge Calibration procedure

## Procedure 4.1 — Recursive Bridge Calibration

Input:

1. a source context \(X\);
2. a target context \(Y\);
3. a target distinction family \(D_Y'\);
4. theorem register \(\mathcal T\);
5. no-go register \(\mathcal N\);
6. current bridge trace / closure-bound record \(\mathcal L\).

Steps:

1. **Parse the claim**
   \[
   X\Rightarrow Y
   \quad\leadsto\quad
   X-\mathfrak B-Y.
   \]

2. **Decompose the target**
   \[
   D_Y'
   =
   D_1\cup\cdots\cup D_k.
   \]

3. **Generate candidate bridges**
   \[
   \mathcal B=\{\mathfrak B_1,\ldots,\mathfrak B_n\}.
   \]

4. **Audit each candidate**
   against the theorem and no-go registers.

5. **Classify outcomes**
   as:
   \[
   \text{success},
   \quad
   \text{partial success},
   \quad
   \text{blocked},
   \quad
   \text{premature},
   \quad
   \text{wrong target},
   \quad
   \text{overclaim risk}.
   \]

6. **Compute blocking/unblocking dependencies**
   among candidates.

7. **Select the next bridge target**
   using dependency and no-go information.

8. **Attempt construction**
   of the selected bridge.

9. **Update trace record**
   with success, partial success, failure, or new no-go.

10. **Recurse**
   with the updated source and trace record.

# 6. Triadic closure-atlas refinement

This section is the **canonical procedural home** for closure-atlas navigation. The companion paper *Triadic Substitution, Coupling, and Closure Atlases* supplies the structural justification; this paper states the operational method.

Recursive Bridge Calibration can now be read as navigation through a triadic closure atlas.

A bridge failure is not only a missing arrow. It may be a missing or unstable role face:

\[
\mathsf Z,
\qquad
\mathsf R,
\qquad
\mathsf O.
\]

It may also be a higher interface failure:

\[
\text{substitution},
\quad
\text{coupling},
\quad
\text{residual},
\quad
\text{trace},
\quad
\text{recursion readout},
\quad
\text{face recovery},
\quad
\text{stranding},
\quad
\text{overclaim}.
\]

## 6.1 Triadic RBC procedure

For each bridge target:

1. identify the active relation-triad locus;
2. identify the missing or unstable role subconfiguration or typed micro-triad;
3. classify whether the failure is singleton, dyadic, or coupling-interface failure;
4. identify which recursion face is active or missing:
   \[
   \mathsf Z\text{-residual},
   \quad
   \mathsf R\text{-trace},
   \quad
   \mathsf O\text{-substitution/readout};
   \]
5. generate candidate role substitutes;
6. check each substitute for local triadic closure;
7. test coupling interfaces for landedness;
8. check whether a declared compatibility operator, face-recovery operation, or typed micro-triad admissibility condition is required;
9. evaluate residual, readout, and admissibility;
10. update the closure-bound record.

## 6.2 Triadic obstruction vector

The canonical triadic obstruction vector for Recursive Bridge Calibration is:

\[
\mathbf o_T
=
(o_{\mathsf Z},o_{\mathsf R},o_{\mathsf O},o_{\rm sub},o_{\rm coup},o_{\rm res},o_{\rm trace},o_{\rm recurread},o_{\rm face},o_{\rm micro},o_{\rm strand},o_{\rm over}).
\]

Where:

- \(o_{\mathsf Z}\): boundary-face obstruction;
- \(o_{\mathsf R}\): mediation-face obstruction;
- \(o_{\mathsf O}\): observer/readout obstruction;
- \(o_{\rm sub}\): role-substitution obstruction;
- \(o_{\rm coup}\): coupling-interface obstruction;
- \(o_{\rm res}\): residual pressure not yet realized;
- \(o_{\rm trace}\): trace/update path missing or inadmissible;
- \(o_{\rm recurread}\): recursion readout/substitution missing;
- \(o_{\rm face}\): compatibility operator or face-recovery operation missing or invalid;
- \(o_{\rm micro}\): typed micro-triad compatibility missing or invalid;
- \(o_{\rm strand}\): stranded or dangling trace obstruction;
- \(o_{\rm over}\): overclaim / terminal-totalization risk.

## 6.3 Relation to the original procedure

This does not replace the original RBC procedure. It refines steps 4--9 of the procedure by making explicit which triadic face or interface is responsible for the obstruction.

The companion closure-atlas paper explains why these components are structurally forced. This paper uses them as the procedure.

A face-recovery failure is present when the bridge treats one triadic face as recoverable from the other two but does not declare the algebraic, order-theoretic, or categorical operation that makes the recovery valid.

A typed micro-triad failure is present when a bridge selects components from boundary, mediation, and readout faces but does not show that the boundary component bounds the mediation component or that the readout reads the mediated distinction.

# 7. Forward calibration rubric

For each bridge candidate, ask:

| Criterion | Question |
|---|---|
| Source | What source distinctions are available? |
| Target | What target distinctions are claimed? |
| Relation | What relation is declared? |
| Boundary | What boundary/admissibility/tolerance is declared? |
| Observer/readout | What makes the target visible or comparable? |
| Recursion face | Is the active recursion residual-, trace-, or substitution-dominant? |
| Coupling interface | Does the bridge land in a closure-complete interface? |
| Compatibility / face recovery | Is a compatibility operator or residual/quotient operation needed and declared? |
| Typed micro-triad | If components are combined, do boundary, mediation, and readout type-check? |
| No-go repaired | Which obstruction does the bridge repair? |
| Remaining no-go | Which obstruction remains? |
| Composition | Does the bridge compose with previous bridges? |
| External template | Does a mature field contain an analogue? |
| Overclaim risk | Does the bridge claim too much? |
| Status | success / partial / blocked / premature / wrong target |

The result is not a numerical score unless one is useful. The central output is a dependency map.

# 8. Blocking and unblocking

## Definition 8.1 — Blocks

A candidate bridge \(\mathfrak B_i\) is **blocked** by a no-go \(N\in\mathcal N\) if:

\[
D_Y'\not\subseteq\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_i}(D_X)
\]

and \(N\) identifies the missing relation, boundary, or readout.

## Definition 8.2 — Unblocks

A candidate bridge \(\mathfrak B_i\) **unblocks** a later candidate \(\mathfrak B_j\) if the target distinctions recovered by \(\mathfrak B_i\) are required as source distinctions for \(\mathfrak B_j\).

Symbolically:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_i}(D_X)
\cap
\operatorname{Req}(\mathfrak B_j)
\neq \varnothing.
\]

## Definition 8.3 — Premature bridge

A bridge \(\mathfrak B_j\) is **premature** if it requires source distinctions not yet supplied by earlier bridges.

Example:

\[
\text{action-to-physical-action}
\]

is premature before:

\[
\text{continuum calibration}
\]

if the target physical variables and domains are not yet available.

# 9. Selection rule

## Definition 9.1 — Next-bridge selection

Given candidate bridges \(\mathcal B\), select a bridge \(\mathfrak B_*\) satisfying:

1. it repairs a current high-priority no-go;
2. it is not premature;
3. it unlocks at least one downstream bridge;
4. it has controlled overclaim risk;
5. it has either a theorem-register template or a mature external analogue;
6. failure would still yield a useful diagnostic.

This is the programme's bridge-selection rule.

It is not optimization for convenience. It is no-go-guided learning.

# 10. Elementary-after-declaration mode

Many of the programme's bridge-repair theorems have an intentionally elementary proof form.

A typical pattern is:

1. a compressed claim uses an undeclared relation;
2. Recursive Bridge Calibration identifies the missing relation, boundary, and readout;
3. the bridge is declared;
4. the target distinction follows directly from the declaration.

This can make the final proof look "trivial by definition." In this programme, that is often the expected successful mode.

The point is not to hide difficult mathematics inside a proof. The point is to force the missing relation into the open so that the success or failure of the bridge becomes checkable.

\[
\boxed{
\text{the theorem becomes elementary because the missing relation has been supplied.}
}
\]

---

## Definition 10.1 — Elementary-after-declaration theorem

A theorem is **elementary-after-declaration** when:

1. the original claim is underdeclared;
2. the calibration procedure identifies the missing bridge data;
3. once the bridge data are declared, the target distinction follows by definition, direct inclusion, or a short verification.

Examples include:

- path-polarity recovery after declaring \((\leadsto,\Omega,\lambda,\operatorname{Adm})\);
- interval counting after declaring local finiteness;
- admissible-history dynamics after declaring a history field, evaluator, and admission rule;
- contextual objecthood after declaring local sections and gluing compatibility.

---

## 10.2 Why this is not a weakness

The objection:

\[
\text{"the result is trivial because it follows from the definition"}
\]

misses the role of the method.

Before calibration, the relevant definition was missing. The compressed claim was doing hidden work. After calibration, the hidden relation has been declared, typed, bounded, and made evaluable.

Thus the elementary proof is evidence that the bridge has been successfully repaired.

The programme's local standard is not:

\[
\boxed{
\text{make every proof technically difficult.}
}
\]

It is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{make every used relation explicit enough that the proof or failure becomes inspectable.}
}
\]

---

## Theorem 10.3 — Bridge repair should simplify verification

If a bridge repair supplies all relation, boundary, and readout data required for a target distinction \(D_Y'\), then verifying recovery of \(D_Y'\) should reduce to a direct check against the declared bridge.

### Proof

Bridge adequacy is defined by:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

If the repair supplies the relation, boundary, readout, and preservation conditions that define \(\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)\), then checking adequacy is precisely checking the inclusion above. This is a direct verification relative to the declared bridge. \(\square\)

---

## 10.4 Not every theorem has this mode

This does not mean every result in the programme must be definitional.

Some results require external mathematics, computation, or empirical testing:

- causal reconstruction theorems;
- TDA stability comparisons;
- modular-cocycle calculations;
- count-volume calibration;
- galaxy morphology/environment tests;
- Standard Model representation constraints;
- physical action or continuum-limit bridges.

So the precise claim is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{elementary-after-declaration is the expected mode of bridge-repair verification, not the only mode of all programme mathematics.}
}
\]

This distinction matters. The programme uses elementary bridge-repair theorems to expose hidden relations, but it still needs nontrivial mathematics and empirical work where the target domain demands them.

# 11. Calibration progress functional

Recursive learning requires an evaluator. In this programme, the evaluator is not a generic loss function imposed from outside. It is built from the programme's own bridge obligations.

A bridge attempt improves the programme when it:

1. recovers a target distinction;
2. repairs a no-go;
3. reduces underdeclared relation;
4. preserves chart/readout visibility;
5. composes with existing successful bridges;
6. lowers overclaim risk;
7. produces a precise failure diagnosis if it fails.

This motivates a general **calibration progress functional**.

---

## Definition 11.1 — Calibration state

A **calibration state** is a tuple:

\[
\mathfrak C_t
=
(\mathcal L_t,D_t,\mathcal O_t,\mathcal R_t)
\]

where:

- \(\mathcal L_t\) is the bridge trace / closure-bound record at stage \(t\);
- \(D_t\) is the current unresolved target distinction family;
- \(\mathcal O_t\) is the current set of open obligations;
- \(\mathcal R_t\) is the current overclaim-risk register.

---

## Definition 11.2 — Obstruction vector

The **obstruction vector** of a calibration state is:

\[
\mathbf o(\mathfrak C_t)
=
(
u_t,
b_t,
c_t,
r_t,
q_t
)
\]

where:

1. \(u_t\) counts undeclared or underdeclared relations;
2. \(b_t\) counts unrepaired no-gos;
3. \(c_t\) counts bridge-composition failures;
4. \(r_t\) counts missing readout/chart-visibility conditions;
5. \(q_t\) counts overclaim risks.

The entries may be literal counts, weighted counts, or ordinal ranks. The only requirement is that they are declared.

---

## Definition 9.3 — Calibration loss

A **calibration loss** is a map:

\[
\mathcal E(\mathfrak C_t)
=
w_u u_t+w_b b_t+w_c c_t+w_r r_t+w_q q_t
\]

with nonnegative declared weights:

\[
w_u,w_b,w_c,w_r,w_q\ge0.
\]

This is the scalar version.

The lexicographic version is:

\[
\mathbf o(\mathfrak C_t)
\prec_{\rm lex}
\mathbf o(\mathfrak C_s).
\]

The lexicographic version is often safer, because it prevents a bridge from reducing a minor count while increasing a major no-go.

---

## Definition 11.4 — Admissible improvement step

A bridge attempt from \(\mathfrak C_t\) to \(\mathfrak C_{t+1}\) is an **admissible improvement step** if either:

\[
\mathcal E(\mathfrak C_{t+1})<\mathcal E(\mathfrak C_t),
\]

or, in the lexicographic version:

\[
\mathbf o(\mathfrak C_{t+1})
\prec_{\rm lex}
\mathbf o(\mathfrak C_t).
\]

A partial success may be an improvement if it reduces some obstruction entries without increasing higher-priority entries.

A failed bridge may also be an improvement if it replaces an ambiguous obstruction with a precise open obligation. In that case the evaluator is not only "distance to success"; it is also "clarity of failure."

---

## Definition 9.5 — Branch-gradient direction

Given candidate bridges:

\[
\mathcal B_t=\{\mathfrak B_1,\ldots,\mathfrak B_n\},
\]

a **branch-gradient direction** is a candidate \(\mathfrak B_*\) whose expected calibration state gives maximal declared improvement:

\[
\mathfrak B_*
\in
\arg\min_{\mathfrak B_i\in\mathcal B_t}
\widehat{\mathcal E}(\mathfrak C_{t+1}^{(i)}).
\]

This is analogous to gradient descent only in role: it selects the next move by expected decrease in obstruction. It is not a differentiable gradient unless the bridge space is given differentiable structure.

---

## Definition 9.6 — Diagnostic convergence

A sequence:

\[
\mathfrak C_0,\mathfrak C_1,\ldots
\]

**diagnostically converges** if it reaches one of:

1. a successful bridge state, where the target distinctions are recovered;
2. a stable partial-success state, where recovered distinctions and remaining no-gos are fully classified;
3. a blocked state, where every available candidate is blocked, premature, or overclaiming and the open obligations are explicit.

This is weaker than truth-convergence. It is the right convergence concept for a foundational research programme.

# 12. Convergence theorems

## Theorem 12.1 — Monotone calibration descent

If every bridge attempt is an admissible improvement step under \(\mathcal E\), then the calibration loss is monotonically non-increasing, and strictly decreasing on successful improvement steps.

### Proof

By Definition 8.4, an admissible improvement step satisfies:

\[
\mathcal E(\mathfrak C_{t+1})<\mathcal E(\mathfrak C_t)
\]

or the corresponding lexicographic decrease. Therefore the sequence of losses decreases at each improvement step. If partial/failure clarification steps are allowed to keep the scalar loss constant while refining the trace record, then the scalar loss is non-increasing and the trace record information increases. \(\square\)

---

## Theorem 12.2 — Finite local termination

Assume:

1. the candidate bridge field \(\mathcal B_t\) is finite at each local stage;
2. the obstruction vector is valued in a well-founded ordered set;
3. every admitted bridge attempt strictly decreases the obstruction vector or terminates as blocked;
4. no candidate is repeated without new trace record information.

Then Recursive Bridge Calibration locally terminates in success, stable partial success, or explicit blockage.

### Proof

A strictly decreasing sequence in a well-founded ordered set cannot continue indefinitely. Since the candidate field is finite and repeated candidates require new trace record information, the local run cannot cycle without progress. Therefore the run terminates in one of the declared terminal states: success, stable partial success, or explicit blockage. \(\square\)

---

## Theorem 12.3 — No truth guarantee

Recursive Bridge Calibration does not guarantee that a selected bridge is physically true or empirically realized.

### Proof

The method evaluates declared relations, recovered target distinctions, no-go repair, and bridge composition. Physical truth or empirical realization requires additional target-world correspondence, measurement, and validation. These are not guaranteed by the formal procedure. \(\square\)

# 13. General theorems

## Theorem 13.1 — No arbitrary next bridge

In a relation-first programme, the next bridge cannot be chosen arbitrarily if candidate bridges have dependency relations.

### Proof

If a bridge \(\mathfrak B_j\) requires source distinctions supplied only by \(\mathfrak B_i\), then attempting \(\mathfrak B_j\) before \(\mathfrak B_i\) leaves its source underdeclared. By No-Undeclared-Relation, the inference is invalid. Therefore dependency relations constrain bridge order. \(\square\)

---

## Theorem 13.2 — Failure is trace record information

If a candidate bridge fails because a no-go identifies a missing relation, boundary, or readout, then the failure updates the bridge trace / closure-bound record with a new open obligation.

### Proof

A failed bridge identifies:

1. the attempted target distinction;
2. the missing bridge component;
3. the no-go responsible for the failure.

These are exactly the data required for \(\mathcal O\), the open-obligation component of the trace record. Therefore failure is not empty; it updates the trace record. \(\square\)

---

## Theorem 11.3 — Partial success composes only through recovered distinctions

A partial-success bridge can be used downstream only through the target distinctions it actually recovered.

### Proof

Let \(\mathfrak B_i\) recover \(D_i'\subsetneq D_Y\). A downstream bridge \(\mathfrak B_j\) can use only source distinctions available from \(\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_i}(D_X)\). Distinctions not in \(D_i'\) were not recovered. Using them would violate No-Free-Transfer. \(\square\)

---

## Theorem 11.4 — Recursive Bridge Calibration terminates locally when no admissible bridge is available

For a fixed finite candidate set \(\mathcal B\), if every candidate is blocked, premature, or overclaiming, then the local calibration run terminates with an open obligation rather than a construction target.

### Proof

The selection rule requires an admissible candidate satisfying the criteria in Definition 7.1. If none exists, no bridge is selected. The failed candidates and their blockers are recorded in the trace record / closure bound as open obligations. \(\square\)

# 16. Approximation as the admissible middle

Recursive Bridge Calibration treats approximation not as a defect but as the normal admissible state of a relation-first programme.

A compressed claim at the initial boundary is underdeclared:

\[
X\Rightarrow Y.
\]

A completed total identity at the terminal boundary would overclaim:

\[
X=Y
\]

or:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)=D_Y
\]

without remainder.

The programme's natural working region is between these two boundaries:

\[
\boxed{
X
-
\mathfrak B
-
Y'
\subseteq
Y.
}
\]

Here, \(Y'\) is the recovered target distinction family, while:

\[
Y\setminus Y'
\]

records the remaining open obligations.

Thus a successful bridge is usually not total identification. It is a calibrated recovery of declared target distinctions under declared tolerances, with unrecovered distinctions explicitly preserved in the trace record.

\[
\boxed{

\boxed{
\text{overclaim / total preservation fantasy.}

The admissible state is neither.

\boxed{
\text{recover exactly.}

# 17. The calibrator role

The previous section says that approximation is admissible when the target, tolerance, and readout are declared. This section makes the missing role explicit:

\[
\boxed{
\text{the calibrator is the observer/readout role for approximation.}
}
\]

A bridge may recover a target distinction only approximately. That approximation is not meaningful until something declares:

1. what is being compared;
2. what tolerance is allowed;
3. which distinctions count as preserved;
4. which failures count as informative;
5. when the bridge is admitted, rejected, or recalibrated.

That role is the **calibrator**.

---

## 15.1 Definition — Calibrator

A **calibrator** for a bridge attempt is a tuple:

\[
\mathfrak K
=
(D_Y',\varepsilon,\mathcal E,\operatorname{Adm},\operatorname{Update})
\]

where:

1. \(D_Y'\) is the declared target distinction family;
2. \(\varepsilon\) is the declared tolerance or admissibility boundary;
3. \(\mathcal E\) is the calibration progress functional;
4. \(\operatorname{Adm}\) is the admission rule for success / partial success / failure;
5. \(\operatorname{Update}\) is the trace-update rule after evaluation.

Thus the calibrator is not merely a score. It is the role that turns approximation into an admissible bridge result.

---

## 15.2 Calibrator as boundary--relation--observer structure

The calibrator itself has the programme's triadic form.

| Calibrator component | Programme role |
|---|---|
| target distinction family \(D_Y'\) | relation target |
| tolerance \(\varepsilon\) | boundary |
| progress functional \(\mathcal E\) | evaluator/readout |
| admission rule \(\operatorname{Adm}\) | observer decision |
| trace update \(\operatorname{Update}\) | recursive trace continuation |

So the calibrator is not external bookkeeping. It is the programme's observer/evaluator structure applied to bridge construction.

---

## 15.3 Calibration functional as evaluator

The calibration functional:

\[
\mathcal E(\mathfrak C_t)
=
w_u u_t+w_b b_t+w_c c_t+w_r r_t+w_q q_t
\]

or its lexicographic version, is the evaluator face of the calibrator.

It measures obstruction relative to the declared target:

\[
(D_Y',\varepsilon).
\]

This matters because the same bridge can be a success relative to one target and a failure relative to another.

Example:

\[
\text{admissible-history dynamics}
\]

is a success relative to:

\[
D_Y'=
\text{formal dynamics-as-selection},
\]

but a failure relative to:

\[
D_Y'=
\text{Einstein field equations}.
\]

The calibrator prevents these from being confused.

## 15.4 Admission rule

A bridge attempt is admitted as:

### Success

if:

\[
\mathcal E_{D_Y',\varepsilon}(\mathfrak C_{t+1})=0
\]

or all declared target distinctions are recovered to tolerance.

### Partial success

if:

\[
\mathcal E_{D_Y',\varepsilon}(\mathfrak C_{t+1})
<
\mathcal E_{D_Y',\varepsilon}(\mathfrak C_t)
\]

and the remaining obstruction is explicit.

### Failure

if the bridge does not recover the declared target and does not reduce obstruction.

### Diagnostic failure

if the bridge does not recover the target but identifies a new precise missing bridge component.

Diagnostic failure updates the trace record even though it is not a success.

It is:

## 15.7 The calibrator theorem

### Theorem 15.7 — Approximate success is admissible only relative to a calibrator

Let \(\mathfrak B\) be a bridge from \(X\) to \(Y\). Suppose \(\mathfrak B\) approximately recovers a target distinction family \(D_Y'\). Then the claim of approximate success is well-typed only if a calibrator:

\[
\mathfrak K
=
(D_Y',\varepsilon,\mathcal E,\operatorname{Adm},\operatorname{Update})
\]

is declared.

### Proof

Approximate recovery requires specifying which target distinctions are being recovered, what tolerance counts as adequate, how the remaining obstruction is evaluated, and whether the result is admitted as success, partial success, or failure. These data are precisely the components of \(\mathfrak K\). Without them, "approximately recovers" is underdeclared. Therefore approximate success is admissible only relative to a declared calibrator. \(\square\)

## 15.8 Calibrator boundedness

The calibrator is itself bounded.

Let:

\[
z_-
\]

denote the initial underdeclaration boundary: no declared relation, no target distinction family, no tolerance, no evaluator, no admission rule, no update rule.

Let:

\[
z_+
\]

denote the terminal totalization boundary: full identity, total preservation, no unrecovered remainder, and no need for calibration.

A valid calibrator lives strictly between these limits:

\[
\boxed{
z_-<\mathfrak K<z_+.
}
\]

This means that the calibrator is neither empty nor totalizing. It declares enough structure to evaluate a bridge, but not so much that bridge work collapses into total identity.

---

## Theorem 15.9 — Calibrator Boundedness Theorem

A calibrator:

\[
\mathfrak K
=
(D_Y',\varepsilon,\mathcal E,\operatorname{Adm},\operatorname{Update})
\]

is admissible only if it lies strictly between the initial underdeclaration boundary \(z_-\) and the terminal totalization boundary \(z_+\).

### Proof

If:

\[
\mathfrak K=z_-,
\]

then no target distinction family \(D_Y'\), tolerance \(\varepsilon\), evaluator \(\mathcal E\), admission rule \(\operatorname{Adm}\), or update rule \(\operatorname{Update}\) is declared. The bridge cannot be evaluated. This is underdeclaration.

If:

\[
\mathfrak K=z_+,
\]

then the calibrator demands total recovery:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)=D_Y
\]

with no unrecovered remainder. Calibration collapses into total identity. This violates the programme's no-totalization discipline and prevents partial recovery from being admitted as learning.

Therefore an admissible calibrator must satisfy:

\[
z_-<\mathfrak K<z_+.
\]

\(\square\)

## Corollary 15.10 — Calibrated Approximation Sufficiency

If a bridge \(\mathfrak B\) recovers the declared target distinction family \(D_Y'\) within the declared tolerance \(\varepsilon\), and the calibrator records unrecovered distinctions in the trace record, then the bridge is sufficient for that calibrated target.

Formally, if:

\[
D_Y'
\subseteq_{\varepsilon}
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X),
\]

and:

\[
D_Y\setminus D_Y'
\]

is recorded in \(\mathcal L\), then \(\mathfrak B\) is admitted as a success or partial success relative to \(\mathfrak K\).

### Proof

The target of calibration is not all of \(D_Y\). It is the declared target distinction family \(D_Y'\) under tolerance \(\varepsilon\). If those distinctions are recovered to tolerance, the declared target has been met. Since unrecovered distinctions are preserved in the trace record, the bridge does not overclaim total recovery. Therefore it is sufficient relative to the calibrator. \(\square\)

## Corollary 15.11 — Exact Local Calibration as Local Boundary

If a bridge \(\mathfrak B\) recovers the declared local target distinction family \(D_Y'\) with zero tolerance error under a declared calibrator \(\mathfrak K\), then the result lies on the local calibration boundary for that target.

Formally, if:

\[
D_Y'
\subseteq_{0}
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X),
\]

then:

\[
\mathcal E_{\mathfrak K}(\mathfrak C_{t+1})=0
\]

relative to \(D_Y'\). The bridge has reached the terminal point of that local calibrated task.

### Proof

The calibrator defines the local target \(D_Y'\), tolerance \(\varepsilon\), evaluator \(\mathcal E\), admission rule, and update rule. If \(\varepsilon=0\) and all distinctions in \(D_Y'\) are recovered, then no obstruction remains relative to that local calibrated target. Hence the calibration loss is zero for \(\mathfrak K\), and the result lies at the local terminal boundary of that task. \(\square\)

### Important qualification

This does not imply:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)=D_Y
\]

for the full target \(D_Y\).

It implies only:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)
\supseteq
D_Y'
\]

with zero tolerance error for the declared local target. The unrecovered remainder:

\[
D_Y\setminus D_Y'
\]

may still be open.

Thus exact local calibration is boundary contact **inside a declared subtask**, not global totalization.

---

## Corollary 15.12 — General Exact Calibration Boundary

If a sequence of bridge attempts recovers every distinction in a declared target family \(D_Y'\) exactly under a calibrator \(\mathfrak K\), then the sequence has reached the calibration boundary for that declared target.

Equivalently:

\[
\mathcal E_{\mathfrak K}(\mathfrak C_t)=0
\]

means:

\[
\boxed{
\text{the declared calibration task is complete.}
}
\]

It does not mean:

\[
\boxed{
\text{all possible targets are complete.}
}
\]

### Proof

The calibration functional is defined relative to \(\mathfrak K\). Therefore zero obstruction means zero obstruction relative to the target, tolerance, evaluator, admission rule, and trace-update rule declared by \(\mathfrak K\). If the target is later enlarged, a new calibrator and a new obstruction vector may open. \(\square\)

Exactness is always indexed to a calibrator.

So the correct form is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{exact for }(D_Y',\mathfrak K)

\]

not simply:

\[
\boxed{
\text{exact absolutely}.

This is why the programme treats exact local success and partial global success as compatible.

This is stronger and safer than saying:

Approximation is enough only when the calibrator makes clear:

3. by what evaluator;
