## Abstract

Foundational arguments often move from one closure context to another. Examples include entropy to geometry, recoverability to causality, positive information geometry to Lorentzian structure, local compatibility to global objecthood, residual structure to dynamics, and prediction to explanation. This paper argues that every such arrow is a relation claim. In a relation-first framework, a relation claim is not valid unless the relation itself is declared, typed, and assigned preservation conditions.

The master principle is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{No declared relation, no legitimate relational inference.}
}
\]

The operative cross-context form is therefore not a bare dyad \(X\to Y\), but a triad:

\[
\boxed{
X
\;-\;
\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}
\;-\;
Y.
}
\]


The cross-context corollary is the No-Free-Transfer theorem. In particular, every apparent dyadic transfer \(X\to Y\) is underdeclared until the mediating bridge relation is supplied, making the operative structure \(X-\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}-Y\). Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be closure contexts with task-visible distinction relations \(D_X\) and \(D_Y\). A cross-context inference

\[
X\Rightarrow Y
\]

is valid only if the \(Y\)-distinctions claimed by the conclusion are recoverable from \(X\)-distinctions under declared bridge data, or if they are formal boundary/definitional consequences common to the framework. Otherwise the inference is a no-free-transfer failure.


The paper also extracts the programme's no-go network: boundary, quotient, triadic, chart, objecthood, learning, residual, and external-transfer no-gos are treated as projections of the same relation-first constraint. This makes the paper more than a bridge taxonomy; it shows how the theorem machinery generated across the companion papers coheres under one primitive discipline.

The constructive method is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{audit}
\quad\to\quad
\text{bridge-type classification}
\quad\to\quad
\text{candidate bridge completion}
\quad\to\quad
\text{calibration}.
}
\]

Given a claim \(X\Rightarrow Y\), the bridge audit chart asks five questions: what is the source context, what is the target context, what target distinctions are claimed, are those target distinctions already encoded in the source, and what bridge data would preserve them if not. The audit does not refute the claim. It makes the claimed relation inspectable.

The paper then applies the method to representative foundations transfers and gives four worked bridge-completion attempts. The strongest quantitative result is the persistence-margin trace-stability theorem: if a path trace's threshold policy remains inside persistence support with perturbation smaller than the support margin, then the trace remains in the proper organization layer. Failure at or beyond the margin identifies the next learning move: recalibration, recharting, or a new threshold policy.

## 0.1 Master principle: no undeclared relation

The bridge in this paper is not an optional explanatory ornament. It is the relation claimed by a cross-context inference. If an argument writes:

\[
X\Rightarrow Y,
\]

then it asserts that a relation carries some structure from \(X\) to \(Y\). In a relation-first framework, that relation cannot remain implicit. It must be declared, typed, and assigned preservation conditions.

Thus No-Free-Transfer is a corollary of the stronger principle:

\[
\boxed{
\textbf{No-Undeclared-Relation.}
}
\]

A cross-context arrow that lacks bridge data is not merely incomplete. It is missing the relation that would make the inference structurally meaningful.

The formal theorem below is elementary by design. Its force is methodological: every foundations arrow must identify what relation performs the transfer and what target distinctions that relation preserves.

## 0.2 Adjacent methodological frameworks

This paper is adjacent to several existing methodological traditions.

- In **analogical reasoning and model transfer**, a source model supplies candidate structure for a target model, but the analogy is not proof. The bridge-template discipline here is similar: a solved bridge supplies a candidate completion only after target adequacy is recalibrated.
- In **negative-results and null-results methodology**, failed attempts can be evidentially valuable when they identify which assumptions fail. The bridge calibration theorem treats failed bridge completion as learning because it localizes the missing preservation component.
- In **Pearl's causal hierarchy**, associational, interventional, and counterfactual claims occupy different closure contexts. The hierarchy is a canonical example of no-free-transfer: counterfactual distinctions do not transfer freely from associational data.
- In **robustness analysis**, a result is more credible when it survives perturbations across models or assumptions. The persistence-margin theorem below is a formal robustness statement for path traces under bounded threshold perturbation.
- In **category-theoretic language**, bridge declarations resemble structure-preserving maps, bridge composition resembles morphism composition, and bridge adequacy resembles preservation of specified structure. No categorical machinery is assumed here, but the analogy clarifies why bridge composition requires compatibility data.

These adjacent frameworks are comparison points, not premises. The paper's internal claim is relation-first: an inference is a relation claim, and undeclared relations do not transfer distinctions.

## 0.3 Diagnostic studies versus worked theorem attempts

Sections 13--18 are **diagnostic case studies**. They apply the audit chart to familiar foundations transfers and identify required bridge types.

Section 21 contains **worked bridge-completion attempts**. These go beyond diagnosis by proving success or failure theorems under declared bridge data. The persistence-margin theorem in §21.4 is the main quantitative cross-paper result of the paper.

## 0.4 Careful-literature versus compressed-slogan convention

When the paper audits a mature field, it targets compressed transfer slogans rather than careful technical literature. For example, the audit of \(\text{entropy}\Rightarrow\text{geometry}\) is not a charge that holographic-entanglement researchers fail to declare bridge data. Responsible work in that field does declare dictionaries, code subspaces, area relations, region classes, reconstruction maps, and dynamical assumptions. The audit instead identifies what must be present whenever the slogan is used.

# 1. Closure contexts and target distinctions

## Definition 1.1 — Closure context

A **closure context** is a structured domain \(X\) equipped with:

\[
\mathfrak X=(X,\mathcal T_X,D_X,\mathcal A_X,\operatorname{Adm}_X),
\]

where:

- \(X\) is a carrier, chart, atlas, organization layer, trace space, residual space, or other structured domain;
- \(\mathcal T_X\) is a declared task family;
- \(D_X\subseteq X\times X\) is the task-visible distinction relation;
- \(\mathcal A_X\) is the admissible structure through which \(X\)-distinctions are read;
- \(\operatorname{Adm}_X\) is the admissibility condition for \(X\)-claims.

If no task family is declared, \(D_X\) is undefined and no nontrivial preservation claim about \(X\)-distinctions is meaningful.

## Definition 1.2 — Target distinction

Let:

\[
\mathfrak Y=(Y,\mathcal T_Y,D_Y,\mathcal A_Y,\operatorname{Adm}_Y)
\]

be a closure context. A **target distinction** is an ordered pair:

\[
(y,y')\in D_Y.
\]

A cross-context claim from \(X\) to \(Y\) is a claim that some family of \(Y\)-target distinctions is determined, preserved, recovered, generated, or constrained by \(X\).

## Definition 1.3 — Source indistinguishability

Given a closure context \(X\), define the source indistinguishability relation:

\[
x\sim_Xx'
\]

when no declared \(X\)-task distinguishes \(x\) from \(x'\).

Equivalently, \(x\sim_Xx'\) iff \(x\) and \(x'\) agree on all declared task-visible \(X\)-distinctions.

## Definition 1.4 — Cross-context inference

A **cross-context inference** is a claim of the form:

\[
\mathfrak X\Rightarrow \mathfrak Y
\]

asserting that structure in \(X\) determines, recovers, preserves, or constrains structure in \(Y\).

A cross-context inference is **nontrivial** if at least one target distinction in \(D_Y\) is not definitional in \(Y\) and not a boundary condition common to the framework.

# 2. Bridge declarations

## 2.0 Source--bridge--target triad

Every cross-context bridge claim has the form:

\[
X \xrightarrow{\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}} Y.
\]

This is not a dyad \(X,Y\). It is a triad:

\[
\boxed{
\text{source context}
\;-\;
\text{declared bridge relation}
\;-\;
\text{target context}.
}
\]

The source context supplies what is available.  
The target context supplies what is claimed.  
The bridge relation supplies the preservation, comparison, recovery, or translation structure by which the target claim can be read from the source.

Thus a bare arrow

\[
X\to Y
\]

is underdeclared unless the middle term is supplied. The bridge is not decorative notation added to an already meaningful transfer. The bridge is the relation that makes the transfer a relation-first claim.

This is the cross-context instance of the programme's triadic discipline:

\[
\boxed{
\text{boundary/source}
+
\text{mediation/bridge}
+
\text{visibility/target-readout}.
}
\]

The source and target alone do not determine what is preserved, what is erased, what is compared, or what counts as successful recovery. The third term is therefore not optional.

### Proposition 2.0.1 — Dyadic transfer is underdeclared

A claimed transfer \(X\to Y\) that names only source and target, but supplies no bridge relation \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\), is not yet a valid cross-context inference.

#### Proof

A nontrivial target claim requires recovery of some target distinction family \(D_Y'\subseteq D_Y\). Recovery is defined only relative to bridge data:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

If no bridge relation \(\mathfrak B\) is declared, then \(\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)\) is undefined. Hence the transfer cannot establish \(D_Y'\). \(\square\)

### Remark 2.0.2 — Binary relations and triadic inference

A bridge may be represented set-theoretically as a binary relation, map, functor, comparison, or transport. This does not make the inference dyadic. The inferential structure remains triadic because the relation is being used to mediate between a source context and a target context:

\[
(X,\mathfrak B_{X\to Y},Y).
\]

The third component is not a third object. It is the declared relation-mode that makes the source and target comparable.



### Remark 2.0.3 — Relation to the triadic mediation inequality

The source--bridge--target triad is the primitive relation-form of the triadic mediation principle. It is prior to any metric or distance assignment.

Once a bridge carries path-cost, residual, accessibility, or comparison structure, the triadic form may be read through a generalized mediation inequality:

\[
\Delta(X,Z)
\preceq
\Delta(X,Y)\otimes \Delta(Y,Z).
\]

When the value structure is the ordinary nonnegative real line with \(\otimes=+\) and \(\preceq=\le\), this becomes the usual triangle inequality:

\[
d(X,Z)\le d(X,Y)+d(Y,Z).
\]

Thus the triangle inequality does not replace No-Undeclared-Relation. Rather, it is a metric realization of the same deeper point: a direct relation is meaningful only inside a declared mediation structure. No-Undeclared-Relation is the primitive constraint; the triangle inequality is one path-cost shadow of that constraint.


## Definition 2.1 — Bridge declaration

A **bridge declaration** from \(\mathfrak X\) to \(\mathfrak Y\) is declared data:

\[
\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}
\]

specifying how \(X\)-structure is read as, transported to, compared with, or recovered as \(Y\)-structure.

Bridge declarations may include:

1. a chart or bridge chart;
2. comparison maps between value spaces;
3. faithful readouts;
4. quotient maps;
5. residual readouts;
6. threshold transport policies;
7. persistence compatibility conditions;
8. task-loss bridges;
9. transition functors;
10. calibration or evaluator compatibility;
11. admissibility relations;
12. recovery maps;
13. gluing or global-section data;
14. action/coupling/update rules.

A bridge declaration is part of the theorem data, not a rhetorical supplement.

## Definition 2.2 — Bridge recovery of distinctions

A bridge declaration \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\) **recovers** a target distinction:

\[
(y,y')\in D_Y
\]

from \(X\) if there are \(X\)-distinctions or \(X\)-admissible data sufficient under \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\) to determine that \(y\) and \(y'\) remain distinguished in \(Y\).

Write:

\[
(y,y')\in \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)
\]

when the \(Y\)-distinction \((y,y')\) is bridge-recoverable from \(D_X\).

## Definition 2.3 — Adequate bridge

A bridge declaration \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\) is **adequate for \(D_Y'\subseteq D_Y\)** if:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

That is, every claimed target distinction is recoverable from source distinctions under the bridge.

## Definition 2.4 — Boundary/definitional distinction

A target distinction is **boundary/definitional** if it follows solely from:

1. initial/terminal boundary discipline;
2. excluded-extrema definitions;
3. formal identity/equality inside the declared context;
4. algebraic rules explicitly defining the context;
5. proper-interior conditions already built into the context.

Boundary/definitional distinctions do not require cross-context bridge data because they are not transferred from \(X\) to \(Y\); they are already part of the declared target context.

## 2.1 Triadic declaration of relation

**Companion-paper note.** The face-corollaries of this section are developed in *No Undeclared Boundary* and *No Undeclared Observer*. The closure-atlas navigation consequences are developed structurally in *Triadic Substitution, Coupling, and Closure Atlases* and operationally in *Recursive Bridge Calibration*.

The source--bridge--target triad is the cross-context expression of a more general declaration law.

A declared relation is not merely a named arrow. It requires a closure-complete triadic role structure:

\[
R_{\rm declared}
\Rightarrow
(\mathsf Z_R,\mathsf R_R,\mathsf O_R).
\]

Where:

- \(\mathsf Z_R\) is the boundary / distinction / admissibility face;
- \(\mathsf R_R\) is the mediation / bridge / transition face;
- \(\mathsf O_R\) is the observer / readout / evaluator face.

Thus No Undeclared Relation has the expanded interpretation:

\[
\boxed{
\text{No relation whose boundary, mediation, or readout face is undeclared.}
}
\]

This does not introduce new primitives over relation. Boundary and observer/readout are necessary faces of declared relation.

### Proposition 2.1.1 — Boundary and observer failures are NUR failures

If a relation claim relies on an undeclared boundary or an undeclared observer/readout, then it violates No Undeclared Relation.

#### Proof

By triadic declaration, a relation is declared only when its boundary, mediation, and observer/readout faces are declared. If the boundary face is missing, the relation's distinction or admissibility condition is underdeclared. If the observer/readout face is missing, the relation's visibility or evaluation condition is underdeclared. In either case the relation itself is underdeclared. \(\square\)

### Remark 2.1.2 — Relation face and mediation

The mediation face is not a fourth principle. It is the relation face governed directly by NUR. No Undeclared Boundary and No Undeclared Observer are therefore face-corollaries of NUR, not sibling master axioms.

# 3. No-Free-Transfer as cross-context corollary

## Theorem 3.1 — No-Free-Transfer

Let:

\[
\mathfrak X=(X,\mathcal T_X,D_X,\mathcal A_X,\operatorname{Adm}_X)
\]

and:

\[
\mathfrak Y=(Y,\mathcal T_Y,D_Y,\mathcal A_Y,\operatorname{Adm}_Y)
\]

be closure contexts. Let \(D_Y'\subseteq D_Y\) be a family of non-boundary, non-definitional target distinctions claimed to follow from \(X\).

If no adequate bridge declaration:

\[
\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}
\]

is supplied such that:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X),
\]

then the inference:

\[
\mathfrak X\Rightarrow \mathfrak Y
\]

does not validly establish \(D_Y'\).

Equivalently:

\[
\boxed{
D_Y'\not\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
X\not\Rightarrow_{D_Y'}Y.
}
\]

### Proof

A non-boundary target distinction \((y,y')\in D_Y'\) is not guaranteed by the target context's definitions or boundary discipline. To establish it from \(X\), the inference must show that \(X\)-structure distinguishes the corresponding target alternatives after translation into \(Y\)-terms.

If no adequate bridge is supplied, then by assumption there is at least one \((y,y')\in D_Y'\) such that:

\[
(y,y')\notin \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

Thus no declared source distinction or source-admissible datum preserves or recovers that target distinction. Therefore the claimed inference fails to establish all target distinctions in \(D_Y'\). Hence:

\[
X\not\Rightarrow_{D_Y'}Y.
\]

\(\square\)


## Remark 3.1.1 — Elementary proof, load-bearing discipline

The proof of Theorem 3.1 is intentionally elementary. The theorem is not presented as a difficult mathematical discovery. It is the formal expression of the No-Undeclared-Relation principle: a cross-context arrow has no structural force unless the relation performing the transfer is declared and shown to preserve the target distinctions.

## Corollary 3.2 — Vocabulary alignment is not transfer

If \(X\) and \(Y\) share vocabulary, analogous notation, similar diagrams, or structurally similar labels, but no adequate bridge declaration is supplied, then no nontrivial cross-context inference follows.

### Proof

Vocabulary alignment alone does not define \(\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)\). Without bridge data, no inclusion:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X)
\]

can be established. Theorem 3.1 applies. \(\square\)

## Corollary 3.3 — Boundary exception

If a target statement is purely boundary/definitional, it may hold without bridge data.

This does not contradict Theorem 3.1 because Theorem 3.1 concerns non-boundary, non-definitional target distinctions.

# 4. Relation to task-visible quotient admissibility

The No-Free-Transfer theorem is the cross-context analogue of the quotient admissibility criterion.

In the quotient-admissibility discipline, a quotient:

\[
q:X\to Q
\]

is task-visible-admissible when:

\[
\boxed{
\sim_q\subseteq\sim_{\mathcal T}^{R}.
}
\]

That is, the quotient may identify only distinctions invisible to the declared task and structure type.

The present theorem says the analogous thing for cross-context inference:

\[
\boxed{
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
}
\]

That is, the target distinctions claimed in \(Y\) must be recoverable from \(X\) under declared bridge data.

Thus:

\[
\boxed{
\text{quotient admissibility}
=
\text{within-context no-free-erasure;}
}
\]

\[
\boxed{
\text{bridge admissibility}
=
\text{cross-context no-free-transfer.}
}
\]

# 5. Bridge adequacy, minimality, and strength

## Definition 5.1 — Bridge strength preorder

Let \(\mathfrak B_1,\mathfrak B_2\) be bridge declarations from \(X\) to \(Y\). Define:

\[
\mathfrak B_1\preceq \mathfrak B_2
\]

iff:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_1}(D_X)
\subseteq
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_2}(D_X).
\]

Thus \(\mathfrak B_2\) is at least as strong as \(\mathfrak B_1\) if it recovers every target distinction recovered by \(\mathfrak B_1\).


This bridge-strength preorder is the cross-context analogue of the resource/preorder discipline used in Paper B. Stronger bridge data recover at least as many target distinctions, just as a more informative representation preserves at least as many task-visible distinctions.

## Proposition 5.2 — Bridge monotonicity

If:

\[
\mathfrak B_1\preceq \mathfrak B_2
\]

and \(\mathfrak B_1\) is adequate for \(D_Y'\), then \(\mathfrak B_2\) is adequate for \(D_Y'\).

### Proof

Adequacy of \(\mathfrak B_1\) gives:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_1}(D_X).
\]

Bridge strength gives:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_1}(D_X)
\subseteq
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_2}(D_X).
\]

Therefore:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_2}(D_X).
\]

So \(\mathfrak B_2\) is adequate. \(\square\)

## Definition 5.3 — Minimal bridge

A bridge declaration \(\mathfrak B\) adequate for \(D_Y'\) is **minimal** if no proper subdeclaration \(\mathfrak B'\subsetneq\mathfrak B\) remains adequate for \(D_Y'\).

Minimality is relative to a declared notion of subdeclaration. It may be by component removal, resource preorder, definitional strength, or preservation strength.

## Proposition 5.4 — Missing component no-go for minimal bridges

If \(\mathfrak B\) is minimal for \(D_Y'\), then every component of \(\mathfrak B\) is load-bearing: removing it destroys adequacy.

### Proof

By definition of minimality, no proper subdeclaration is adequate. Removing any component produces a proper subdeclaration. Therefore the resulting bridge is inadequate. \(\square\)

# 6. Bridge composition and incompatibility

## Definition 6.1 — Composable bridges

Let:

\[
\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}
\]

and:

\[
\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}
\]

be bridge declarations. They are **composable** if the target distinctions recovered in \(Y\) by \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\) include all source distinctions required by \(\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}\).

Equivalently, if \(D_Y^{\rm req}\) denotes the \(Y\)-distinctions needed to activate \(\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}\), then:

\[
D_Y^{\rm req}
\subseteq
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}}(D_X).
\]

## Definition 6.2 — Composite bridge

When \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\) and \(\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}\) are composable, their composite is:

\[
\mathfrak B_{X\to Z}
=
\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}\circ \mathfrak B_{X\to Y}.
\]

Its recovered distinctions are:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{X\to Z}}(D_X)
=
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}}
\left(
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}}(D_X)
\right).
\]

## Theorem 6.3 — Bridge composition theorem

If:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}}(D_X)
\]

and:

\[
D_Z'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}}(D_Y'),
\]

then:

\[
D_Z'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{X\to Z}}(D_X).
\]

### Proof

By the first inclusion, the relevant \(Y\)-distinctions are recoverable from \(X\). By the second inclusion, the target \(Z\)-distinctions are recoverable from those \(Y\)-distinctions. By definition of composite bridge recovery:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{X\to Z}}(D_X)
=
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}}
\left(
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}}(D_X)
\right).
\]

Therefore \(D_Z'\) is contained in the composite recovery set. \(\square\)

## Definition 6.4 — Bridge incompatibility

Two bridges:

\[
\mathfrak B_{X\to Y},
\qquad
\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}
\]

are **incompatible** if \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\) fails to recover at least one distinction required by \(\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}\).

## Corollary 6.5 — Bridge incompatibility blocks composition

If \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\) and \(\mathfrak B_{Y\to Z}\) are incompatible, then the composite inference:

\[
X\Rightarrow Z
\]

cannot be validly established through those bridges.

### Proof

By incompatibility, a required \(Y\)-distinction is absent from:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}}(D_X).
\]

Thus the hypothesis of Theorem 6.3 fails. Therefore no valid composite bridge inference follows. \(\square\)

# 7. The bridge audit chart

## Definition 7.1 — Transfer claim

A **transfer claim** is an asserted inference:

\[
X\Rightarrow Y
\]

from a source closure context \(X\) to a target closure context \(Y\).

The claim may be explicit or compressed in prose. Examples include:

\[
\text{entropy}\Rightarrow\text{geometry},
\]

\[
\text{recoverability}\Rightarrow\text{causality},
\]

\[
\text{positive information metric}\Rightarrow\text{Lorentzian metric},
\]

\[
\text{local compatibility}\Rightarrow\text{global objecthood}.
\]

## Definition 7.2 — Bridge audit chart

The **bridge audit chart** for a transfer claim \(X\Rightarrow Y\) is the five-question relation-map:

\[
\mathcal C_{\rm audit}(X\Rightarrow Y)
=
(Q_1,Q_2,Q_3,Q_4,Q_5),
\]

where:

\[
Q_1=\text{source context identification},
\]

\[
Q_2=\text{target context identification},
\]

\[
Q_3=\text{target distinction identification},
\]

\[
Q_4=\text{source encoding test},
\]

\[
Q_5=\text{missing bridge classification}.
\]

The audit chart is itself a chart in the programme's sense: it is a relation-map that makes the transfer claim locally legible by separating source, target, target distinctions, source encoding, and required bridge data.

## Proposition 7.3 — Audit chart as bridge declaration precursor

A bridge audit chart does not itself supply bridge data. It supplies the chart in which bridge data can be named.

### Proof

The five audit questions identify the source context, target context, and target distinctions. They do not construct a recovery, readout, comparison map, chartability map, faithful loss, or transport relation. Therefore the audit chart is prior to a bridge declaration. It makes the missing bridge visible but does not fill it. \(\square\)

# 8. Bridge as relation, path, trace, resolution, and closure bound

A bridge is not an external explanatory ornament. It is relation-first.

## Definition 8.1 — Bridge relation

A **bridge relation** from \(X\) to \(Y\) is a relation-class:

\[
\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}
\]

whose task is to preserve or recover declared \(Y\)-distinctions from declared \(X\)-distinctions.

## Definition 8.2 — Bridge path

A **bridge path** is a directed sequence of bridge relations:

\[
X=X_0
\xrightarrow{\mathfrak B_0}
X_1
\xrightarrow{\mathfrak B_1}
\cdots
\xrightarrow{\mathfrak B_{n-1}}
X_n=Y.
\]

## Definition 8.3 — Bridge trace

A **bridge trace** is an admitted bridge path together with its declared preservation data, evaluator, or adequacy test.

Thus a bridge trace records not only that \(X\) was related to \(Y\), but how the relation preserved the target distinctions.

## Definition 8.4 — Bridge resolution

A bridge resolves a transfer claim when the target distinctions \(D_Y'\) are recovered:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

## Definition 8.5 — Closure bound of a bridge

The **closure bound** of a bridge is the limit of what the bridge can preserve. Formally, it is the maximal target distinction family \(D_Y^{\max}\subseteq D_Y\) such that:

\[
D_Y^{\max}\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

A bridge may resolve one target family and fail another.

## Proposition 8.6 — Bridge relation principle

Every legitimate bridge declaration is a relation-class, path, trace, resolution, or closure bound, depending on the level of description.

### Proof

A bridge declaration relates source distinctions to target distinctions. As a relation, it is a bridge relation. If the relation is decomposed across intermediate contexts, it is a bridge path. If admitted and recorded with preservation data, it is a bridge trace. If adequate for a target distinction family, it is a bridge resolution. Its maximal preservation scope is its closure bound. These are not separate primitives; they are faces of one bridge structure. \(\square\)

# 9. Bridge template transfer

The constructive method begins when a solved case and an underdeclared case share a bridge profile.

## Definition 9.1 — Bridge profile

A **bridge profile** is a tuple:

\[
\Pi(\mathfrak B)
=
(S,T,D_T,\mathcal R,\mathcal A),
\]

where:

- \(S\) is the source type;
- \(T\) is the target type;
- \(D_T\) is the target distinction family;
- \(\mathcal R\) is the recovery/readout/comparison form;
- \(\mathcal A\) is the adequacy condition.

## Definition 9.2 — Solved bridge template

A **solved bridge template** is a bridge declaration \(\mathfrak B^\star\) whose adequacy for its target distinctions has been proved or accepted in a mature theory.

Examples include:

- causal-order plus volume in causal set continuum approximation;
- intervention structure in structural causal models;
- recovery maps in quantum approximate recovery;
- gluing/global sections in sheaf contextuality;
- action/coupling rules in variational dynamics.

## Definition 9.3 — Bridge-profile match

An underdeclared transfer \(X\Rightarrow Y\) has a **bridge-profile match** with a solved bridge \(\mathfrak B^\star\) if there are structure-type maps:

\[
\Phi_S:S_X\to S_{\star}
\]

and:

\[
\Phi_T:T_Y\to T_{\star}
\]

such that the target distinction types and adequacy conditions correspond.

This does not establish the bridge. It identifies a candidate template.

## Proposition 9.4 — Bridge-template transfer is candidate, not proof

If \(\mathfrak B^\star\) is a solved bridge template with a matching bridge profile for \(X\Rightarrow Y\), then \(\mathfrak B^\star\) supplies candidate bridge data for \(X\Rightarrow Y\). It does not establish adequacy unless the translated bridge satisfies:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

### Proof

A profile match identifies structural similarity between bridge tasks. But adequacy is defined by recovery of target distinctions in the target case. Unless that inclusion is verified, the transfer remains a candidate. \(\square\)

# 10. Calibration of bridge completions

Bridge completion is a learning process.

## Definition 10.1 — Bridge completion attempt

A **bridge completion attempt** is a candidate bridge declaration \(\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}\) proposed for an underdeclared transfer.

## Definition 10.2 — Bridge calibration

A bridge completion attempt is **calibrated** against a target distinction family \(D_Y'\) by checking:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

If the inclusion holds, the bridge succeeds for \(D_Y'\). If it fails, the bridge failure is informative: it identifies distinctions not preserved by the candidate bridge.

## Proposition 10.3 — Failed bridge calibration is valid learning

If a bridge completion attempt fails for \(D_Y'\), then the failure is not meaningless. It identifies a missing preservation component or a mismatch between the solved bridge template and the underdeclared transfer.

### Proof

Failure of bridge adequacy means there exists:

\[
(y,y')\in D_Y'
\]

such that:

\[
(y,y')\notin \operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

This identifies a target distinction not preserved by the attempted bridge. The failure therefore refines the bridge profile or shows that the template is inadequate. \(\square\)

# 11. No-go network generated by No Undeclared Relation

This section makes explicit a structure that is implicit across the companion programme.

The principle

Some no-gos sit on shared plains; some are incomparable; some are projections of the same primitive failure through different charted contexts.

## 11.1 Closure-depth warning

A no-go network is not a numbered ladder:

\[
L_0<L_1<L_2<\cdots.
\]

It is better represented by a closure-depth preorder:

\[
X\preceq_{\rm cl}Y,
\]

meaning that \(Y\) is obtainable from \(X\) by declared operations, traces, charting, bridge declarations, residual readouts, compression, or closure. Two structures may be closure-equivalent, incomparable, or mutually reachable through different traces.

Therefore the levels below are organizational strata, not ontological ranks. A theorem appearing at a later stratum is not necessarily more real, more fundamental, or less primitive. It may simply require more declared relation structure.

## 11.2 Relation-work checklist

Any asserted arrow

\[
A\to B
\]

is a relation claim. In this programme, such a claim is legitimate only when the relation declares at least:

1. source context \(A\);
2. target context \(B\);
3. source distinctions \(D_A\);
4. target distinctions \(D_B\);
5. preservation or recovery map;
6. admissibility condition;
7. failure mode;
8. boundary/definitional exceptions, if any.

If any of these are absent, the arrow is underdeclared. It may still be a useful heuristic, but it is not yet a valid relation-first inference.

## 11.3 No total preservation under proper transfer

The following theorem extracts a recurring pattern from Paper B, Charted Organization, Recursive Chart Learning, Cross-Closure, and the present note.

### Definition 11.3.1 — Proper transfer

Let \(\mathfrak B_{A\to B}\) be a bridge relation from closure context \(A\) to closure context \(B\). It is a **proper transfer** relative to \(D_A\) if it is not a \(D_A\)-equivalence; that is, at least one source distinction in \(D_A\) is not recoverable in \(B\) under the bridge.

Equivalently:

\[
D_A\not\subseteq\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B^{-1}}(D_B),
\]

when the reverse recovery expression is defined.

A quotient, compression, coarse chart, lossy bridge, or task-relative reduction is a proper transfer when it collapses at least one source distinction.

### Definition 11.3.2 — Total preservation

A bridge \(\mathfrak B_{A\to B}\) is **totally preserving relative to \(D_A\)** if every source distinction is recoverable on the target side:

\[
D_A\subseteq\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B^{-1}}(D_B).
\]

### Theorem 11.3 — No total preservation under proper transfer

A bridge relation cannot be both a proper transfer relative to \(D_A\) and totally preserving relative to \(D_A\).

### Proof

A proper transfer relative to \(D_A\) means at least one source distinction in \(D_A\) is not recoverable on the target side. Total preservation relative to \(D_A\) means every source distinction in \(D_A\) is recoverable on the target side. These conditions are contradictory. \(\square\)

### Remark 11.3.3 — Relation to Paper B quotient admissibility

Theorem 11.3 is the cross-context version of Paper B's quotient discipline. Paper B states that a quotient is admissible only when it collapses no distinction visible to the declared task and structure type:

\[
\sim_q\subseteq\sim_{\mathcal T}^{R}.
\]

Equivalently, a quotient may be lossy only relative to distinctions the task has declared irrelevant. Theorem 11.3 generalizes the same discipline to bridge relations: a proper transfer is allowed to be lossy, but it cannot simultaneously be described as totally preserving the source distinctions. If a claimed transfer preserves all source distinctions, it should be classified as equivalence, enrichment, embedding, or wrong-direction relation rather than as a proper lossy transfer.

### Corollary 11.4 — Direction diagnostic

If a claimed relation \(A\to B\) preserves every \(A\)-distinction and \(B\) contains additional distinctions not recoverable from \(A\), then the relation is not a proper compression of \(A\) into \(B\). It is an embedding, enrichment, equivalence-with-extra-structure, or a relation whose claimed direction requires additional justification.

In particular, a theory claiming that \(A\) produces \(B\) must show whether:

1. \(B\) is a lossy/proper transfer of \(A\);
2. \(B\) is an enriched extension of \(A\);
3. \(A\) and \(B\) are equivalent relative to the declared task;
4. the relation actually runs in the opposite or a bidirectional direction;
5. extra bridge data supply the additional \(B\)-distinctions.

This corollary is one of the strongest practical uses of No Undeclared Relation: the direction of a relation is itself a declared structural claim, not a free arrow.

## 14.3 Solved bridge templates

The constructive conclusion is not that entropy-to-geometry programmes fail. It is that compressed claims of the form:

\[
\text{entropy}\Rightarrow\text{geometry}
\]

must be expanded into their bridge declarations before they are inspectable. In careful holographic work, much of this bridge data is explicitly supplied: dictionary, region class, code subspace, area relation, reconstruction theorem, and dynamical setting. The audit is therefore a vulnerability check on compressed slogans, not a critique of responsible technical literature.

\[
\boxed{
\text{an entropy-to-geometry derivation is inspectable exactly to the extent that its geometric bridge data are declared.}
}
\]

The purpose is not to prove that all external transfers succeed. The purpose is to show how the No-Free-Transfer theorem becomes constructive: when the bridge succeeds, the target distinctions are recovered; when the bridge fails, the failure identifies the missing bridge data.

Each worked attempt should be read as a source--bridge--target triad:

\[
\boxed{
X
\;-\;
\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}
\;-\;
Y.
}
\]

The examples are included not merely to name missing bridges, but to show that the bridge is the necessary mediating relation. Source and target alone do not specify what is preserved, compared, recovered, or erased.

## 22.1 Attempt I — Residual to learning pressure

**Triadic form.**

\[
\boxed{
\Omega
\;-\;
\mathfrak B_{\Omega\to L}
\;-\;
L_\tau.
}
\]

The residual context is the source, learning pressure is the target, and the faithful residual-to-loss/evaluator/admission bridge is the mediating relation.

### Source context

Let:

\[
X=\Omega
\]

be a residual context. Its source distinctions are residual distinctions:

\[
D_X=
\{(\Omega_1,\Omega_2):\Omega_1\not\equiv_\Omega\Omega_2\}.
\]

### Target context

Let:

\[
Y=L_\tau
\]

be a learning-pressure context for a task \(\tau\). Its target distinctions include:

\[
D_Y=
\{
\text{admitted vs. non-admitted},
\text{ improving vs. regressive},
\text{ traceable vs. non-traceable}
\}.
\]

### Candidate bridge

Declare a residual-to-learning bridge:

\[
\mathfrak B_{\Omega\to L}
=
(\operatorname{Loss}_\tau,E_\tau,W_\tau^{\rm adm},X_\tau),
\]

where:

- \(\operatorname{Loss}_\tau\) is a faithful readout of residual structure;
- \(E_\tau\) is an evaluator induced by the loss, for example by order reversal;
- \(W_\tau^{\rm adm}\) is the admitted evaluator region;
- \(X_\tau\) is the declared branch or trace domain.

### Calibration test

The bridge is adequate for a target distinction family \(D_Y'\subseteq D_Y\) only if:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{\Omega\to L}}(D_X).
\]

In concrete terms, if two residuals satisfy:

\[
\Omega_1\not\equiv_\Omega \Omega_2,
\]

then the bridge transfers that distinction to learning pressure only when:

\[
\operatorname{Loss}_\tau(\Omega_1)
\neq
\operatorname{Loss}_\tau(\Omega_2)
\]

and that difference is visible to \(E_\tau\) and relevant to \(W_\tau^{\rm adm}\) or the outcome classes.

### Success theorem

#### Theorem 22.1 — Faithful residual readout induces learning-pressure distinction

Suppose:

1. \((\Omega_1,\Omega_2)\in D_X\);
2. \(\operatorname{Loss}_\tau\) is faithful for the residual distinction, so
   \[
   \Omega_1\not\equiv_\Omega \Omega_2
   \Rightarrow
   \operatorname{Loss}_\tau(\Omega_1)
   \neq
   \operatorname{Loss}_\tau(\Omega_2);
   \]
3. \(E_\tau\) is induced by \(\operatorname{Loss}_\tau\) and preserves the relevant ordering or class separation;
4. the induced evaluator values lie in declared admission/outcome regions.

Then the residual distinction transfers to a learning-pressure distinction.

### Proof

By faithfulness, the residual distinction is visible as a loss distinction. Since \(E_\tau\) is induced by the loss and preserves the declared ordering or class separation, the loss distinction is visible as an evaluator distinction. If the evaluator distinction intersects the admission or outcome classes, it determines a learning-pressure distinction. Hence the target learning-pressure distinction lies in:

\[
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{\Omega\to L}}(D_X).
\]

\(\square\)

### Failure theorem

#### Theorem 22.2 — Non-faithful residual readout blocks learning-pressure transfer

If:

\[
\Omega_1\not\equiv_\Omega\Omega_2
\]

but:

\[
\operatorname{Loss}_\tau(\Omega_1)
=
\operatorname{Loss}_\tau(\Omega_2),
\]

then this residual distinction does not transfer to learning pressure through \(\operatorname{Loss}_\tau\).

### Proof

The candidate bridge collapses the residual distinction at the readout stage. Since the evaluator is induced from the loss, no evaluator distinction can recover a distinction already erased by the loss unless additional bridge data are supplied. Therefore:

\[
(\Omega_1,\Omega_2)\notin
\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{\Omega\to L}}(D_X)
\]

for the claimed learning-pressure distinction. \(\square\)

### Diagnosis

The failed bridge identifies the missing component:

\[
\boxed{
\text{a faithful residual-to-loss readout is missing.}
}
\]

This is a theory-native learning result. A nonzero residual is not yet learning pressure. It becomes learning pressure only under a faithful readout/evaluator/admission bridge.

\]

be the family of local proper organization layers over an observer atlas.

### Target context

Let:

\[
Y=\operatorname{Obj}(\mathfrak A_R)

be global objecthood: compatible charted organization across the atlas.

Target distinctions include:

\[
D_Y=
\{
\text{compatible family vs. no compatible family},
\text{objecthood vs. local fixation}

Declare a gluing bridge:

\[
\mathfrak B_{\rm glue}
=

where:

- \(\mathsf{Ctx}_{\mathfrak A}\) is the chart context category;
- \(\mathcal F_{\operatorname{Org}}\) assigns each chart its proper organization layer;
- \(\rho_{\mathcal C\mathcal D}\) are restriction or transport maps;

#### Theorem 22.3 — Gluing bridge turns local compatibility into objecthood

(O_{\mathcal C})_{\mathcal C\in\mathbf C_R}
\]

satisfies:

\[
\rho_{\mathcal C\mathcal D}(O_{\mathcal D})=O_{\mathcal C}
\]

for every declared restriction or transport relation, then the family defines a charted object.

### Proof

The stated condition is exactly the compatible-family condition for the organization presheaf or transport functor. By the charted objecthood definition, compatible families of local proper organizations are charted objects. \(\square\)

### Failure theorem

#### Theorem 22.4 — Local proper organization does not transfer to objecthood without gluing

If every chart \(\mathcal C\) has at least one local proper organization but no compatible family exists across declared transitions, then local compatibility does not transfer to global objecthood.

### Proof

Objecthood requires a compatible family. Local nonemptiness gives:

\[
\operatorname{Org}^{\circ}_{\theta_{\mathcal C}}(\mathcal C)\neq\varnothing
\]

for every chart, but this does not imply that a family can be chosen satisfying all transition constraints. If no such family exists, the target objecthood distinction is not recovered. The No-Free-Transfer theorem applies. \(\square\)

If a cycle \(\gamma\) in the chart context induces holonomy:

\[
H_\gamma:\operatorname{Org}^{\circ}_{\theta_{\mathcal C}}(\mathcal C)
\to
\operatorname{Org}^{\circ}_{\theta_{\mathcal C}}(\mathcal C)
\]

and:

\[
H_\gamma(O)\neq O
\]

for every local candidate \(O\), then no compatible object family can have base value \(O\).

Thus the missing bridge component is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{flat or fixed-point-compatible gluing transport.}
}
\]

## 22.3 Attempt III — Positive information geometry to Lorentzian structure

**Triadic form.**

\boxed{
G_+
\;-\;
\mathfrak B_{+\to L}
\;-\;
G_L.

\]

Positive information geometry is the source, Lorentzian structure is the target, and the required order/indefinite/analytic/signature bridge is the mediating relation.

### Source context

Let:

\[
X=G_+
\]

be a positive or positive-semidefinite information-geometric context, such as one generated by Fisher-type, Bures-type, relative-entropy-Hessian, or positive recoverability geometry.

Its source distinctions are distinguishability or recovery-loss distinctions.

### Target context

Let:

\[
Y=G_L
\]

be Lorentzian metric structure. Its target distinctions include:

\[
D_Y=
\{
\text{indefinite signature},
\text{time-like direction},
\text{causal cones},
\text{conformal causal order},
\text{metric scale}
\}.
\]

### No-free-transfer diagnosis

Positive distinguishability data do not encode negative directions under real nondegenerate reparametrization. Therefore:

\[
G_+\not\Rightarrow G_L
\]

without additional bridge data.

### Candidate bridge types

Possible solved bridge templates include:

1. **causal/order bridge**:
   causal order or precedence structure;
2. **order-volume bridge**:
   causal order plus volume/cardinality data;
3. **Wick/analytic bridge**:
   analytic continuation or signature-changing operation;
4. **Krein/indefinite bridge**:
   indefinite pairing or Lorentzian spectral structure;
5. **hyperbolic-dynamical bridge**:
   propagation equations or domain-of-dependence structure.

### Failure theorem

#### Theorem 22.5 — Positive information geometry alone does not recover Lorentzian target distinctions

Let \(G_+\) supply only positive or positive-semidefinite quadratic-form distinctions. Let \(D_Y'\subseteq D_Y\) include indefinite signature. If no bridge supplies causal, indefinite, analytic, Wick, Krein, order-volume, or hyperbolic structure, then:

\[
D_Y'\not\subseteq\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B}(D_X).
\]

Therefore \(G_+\not\Rightarrow_{D_Y'}G_L\).

### Proof

A positive-semidefinite quadratic form has no negative direction. Under real nondegenerate reparametrization, the inertia of a symmetric bilinear form is preserved. Therefore an indefinite Lorentzian signature is not recoverable from positive quadratic-form data alone. Since no bridge supplies an indefinite/order-like/signature-changing ingredient, the target distinction is not recovered. The No-Free-Transfer theorem applies. \(\square\)

### Conditional success theorem

#### Theorem 22.6 — An order/indefinite bridge can make Lorentzian transfer inspectable

If a bridge declaration \(\mathfrak B_{+\to L}\) supplies an order-like or indefinite structure sufficient to recover the target Lorentzian distinction family \(D_Y'\), then the transfer:

\[
G_+ + \mathfrak B_{+\to L}\Rightarrow G_L
\]

is inspectable by checking:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_{+\to L}}(D_X).
\]

### Proof

This is bridge adequacy. Once the bridge supplies the target distinctions and the inclusion is verified, the cross-context inference is no longer free transfer. \(\square\)

### Diagnosis

The failure is not “information geometry is useless.” The failure is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{positive distinguishability does not itself provide Lorentzian signature.}
}
\]

The next step is to identify and declare the first causal, indefinite, analytic, or order-volume ingredient.

## 22.4 Attempt IV — Featured quantitative theorem: persistence margin to trace stability

**Triadic form.**

\[
\boxed{
P
\;-\;
\mathfrak B_{P\to T}
\;-\;
T_\tau.
}
\]

Persistence support is the source, path-trace stability is the target, and the persistence-margin/threshold-policy/transport bridge is the mediating relation.

This attempt is the quantitative bridge-completion example and the strongest theorem-grade success case in this paper. It is the quantitative version of the persistence-compatible trace bridge from Cross-Closure Instantiation Theorems: persistence support comes from the Theory of Organization, charted organization from Charted Organization Theory, path traces and threshold policies from Recursive Chart Learning, and residual perturbations from Triadic Closure / Cross-Closure.

### Source context

Let:

\[
X=P
\]

be a persistence-support context. A local organization \(O_i\) in chart \(\mathcal C_i\) has a persistence support interval:

\[
I_i=(\ell_i,u_i]
\]

inside a metric zero-boundary value space \(Z_{\mathcal C_i}\).

The source distinctions are persistence distinctions:

\[
D_X=
\{
\theta\in I_i
\text{ vs. }
\theta\notin I_i
\}.
\]

### Target context

Let:

\[
Y=T_\tau
\]

be a learning path-trace context. The target distinction is:

\[
\text{trace remains proper}
\quad\text{vs.}\quad
\text{trace exits the proper organization layer}.
\]

A path trace is:

\[
p:
\mathcal C_0
\xrightarrow{\phi_0}
\mathcal C_1
\xrightarrow{\phi_1}
\cdots
\xrightarrow{\phi_{n-1}}
\mathcal C_n.
\]

A threshold policy supplies:

\[
\Theta_\tau(p)=(\theta_0,\ldots,\theta_n).
\]

At each chart \(\mathcal C_i\), the transported organization is proper when:

\[
\theta_i\in I_i.
\]

### Candidate bridge

Declare a persistence-to-trace bridge:

\[
\mathfrak B_{P\to T}
=
(I_i,\Theta_\tau,p,\delta_i,\operatorname{Trans}_i),
\]

where:

- \(I_i=(\ell_i,u_i]\) is the persistence support of the transported organization at \(\mathcal C_i\);
- \(\Theta_\tau(p)\) is the threshold schedule along the trace;
- \(\delta_i\) is a residual perturbation or threshold displacement at step \(i\);
- \(\operatorname{Trans}_i\) is the declared transport identifying the organization at adjacent charts.

Define the persistence margin:

\[
m_i
=
\operatorname{dist}(\theta_i,\partial I_i)
=
\min(\theta_i-\ell_i,u_i-\theta_i)
\]

when \(Z_{\mathcal C_i}\) is realized as an interval in \(\mathbb R\).

For half-open supports \((\ell_i,u_i]\), the lower boundary is excluded and the upper boundary is included. Therefore exact boundary cases require the convention:

\[
\theta_i-\delta_i>\ell_i,
\qquad
\theta_i+\delta_i\le u_i.
\]

The strict version

\[
|\delta_i|<m_i
\]

is sufficient and avoids endpoint ambiguity.

### Success theorem

#### Theorem 22.7 — Persistence margin stabilizes proper learning traces

Let \(p\) be a path trace with transported organizations \(O_i\) and threshold schedule:

\[
\Theta_\tau(p)=(\theta_0,\ldots,\theta_n).
\]

Assume:

1. each \(O_i\) has persistence support \(I_i=(\ell_i,u_i]\);
2. each threshold lies inside its support:
   \[
   \theta_i\in I_i;
   \]
3. residual perturbations satisfy:
   \[
   |\delta_i|<m_i
   =
   \min(\theta_i-\ell_i,u_i-\theta_i)
   \]
   for every \(i\);
4. the transport maps identify the same path-traced organization along \(p\).

Then the perturbed threshold schedule:

\[
\Theta'_\tau(p)
=
(\theta_0+\delta_0,\ldots,\theta_n+\delta_n)
\]

also remains inside the persistence supports:

\[
\theta_i+\delta_i\in I_i
\]

for every \(i\). Therefore the path trace remains in the proper organization layer at every step.

### Proof

For each \(i\), the margin condition gives:

\[
|\delta_i|<\theta_i-\ell_i
\]

and:

\[
|\delta_i|<u_i-\theta_i.
\]

Thus:

\[
\theta_i+\delta_i>\ell_i
\]

and:

\[
\theta_i+\delta_i<u_i
\]

or, under the half-open endpoint convention, certainly:

\[
\theta_i+\delta_i\le u_i.
\]

Therefore:

\[
\theta_i+\delta_i\in(\ell_i,u_i]=I_i.
\]

Since this holds for every \(i\), every transported organization \(O_i\) remains proper at its perturbed threshold. Hence the whole trace remains inside the proper organization layer. \(\square\)

### Failure theorem

#### Theorem 22.8 — Crossing the persistence margin blocks guaranteed trace stability

If for some \(i\):

\[
|\delta_i|\ge m_i,
\]

then persistence support alone no longer guarantees that:

\[
\theta_i+\delta_i\in I_i.
\]

Thus the persistence-to-trace bridge does not guarantee proper trace preservation at step \(i\).

### Proof

By definition:

\[
m_i=\min(\theta_i-\ell_i,u_i-\theta_i).
\]

If \(|\delta_i|\ge m_i\), then there exists a perturbation direction that reaches or crosses one boundary of \(I_i\). For negative perturbation it may reach \(\ell_i\), which is excluded. For positive perturbation it may exceed \(u_i\). Therefore support membership is no longer guaranteed by the declared margin. \(\square\)

### Recursive learning interpretation

This attempt is a concrete instance of Recursive Chart Learning.

A path trace is admitted under a threshold policy. If residual perturbations remain below the persistence margin, the trace stays viable and can update the future available step field. If the perturbation crosses the margin, the trace may exit proper organization and the update loses its admissibility guarantee.

Thus the bridge produces a learning diagnosis:

\[
\boxed{
|\delta_i|<m_i
\Rightarrow
\text{trace viability is stable at step }i.
}
\]

\[
\boxed{
|\delta_i|\ge m_i
\Rightarrow
\text{next learning step requires recalibration, recharting, or a new threshold policy.}
}
\]

This is a quantitative example of the programme's learning loop:

\[
\text{path trace}
\to
\text{evaluation by persistence margin}
\to
\text{success or failure}
\to
\text{updated bridge/threshold policy}.
\]

### Worked numerical example

Let a transported organization have support:

\[
I=(0.2,0.9].
\]

Let:

\[
\theta=0.5.
\]

Then:

\[
m=\min(0.5-0.2,0.9-0.5)=\min(0.3,0.4)=0.3.
\]

If:

\[
|\delta|=0.1,
\]

then:

\[
0.4\in(0.2,0.9],
\qquad
0.6\in(0.2,0.9],
\]

so the trace remains proper.

If:

\[
\delta=-0.31,
\]

then:

\[
\theta+\delta=0.19\notin(0.2,0.9],
\]

so the trace exits the persistence support. The failure identifies the missing next move: recalibrate the threshold policy, rechart the organization, or supply a different persistence bridge.



# 23. Branch traces for bridge completion

The worked attempts above show individual bridge calibrations. This section makes the branching structure explicit.

Every branch in a bridge-completion trace is a candidate middle term in a source--bridge--target triad. A branch succeeds when that middle term adequately recovers the target distinctions; it fails when that middle term is absent, ill-typed, too weak, non-faithful, or oriented in the wrong direction.

This is not a repetition of the No-Free-Transfer theorem. No-Free-Transfer states that a nontrivial target distinction cannot transfer without adequate bridge data. A **bridge-completion branch trace** records the actual sequence of candidate bridges tried, the success or failure of each branch, and the diagnostic repair indicated by each failure.

Thus the branch-trace layer answers a different question:

\[
\boxed{
\text{given a missing bridge, what candidate repairs are tried, and what does each result teach?}
}
\]

---

## 23.1 Bridge-completion branch

A **bridge-completion branch** for a transfer claim \(X\Rightarrow Y\) is a tuple

\[
b=(\mathfrak B_b,D_Y',\operatorname{Test}_b,\operatorname{Out}_b),
\]

where:

- \(\mathfrak B_b\) is a candidate bridge declaration;
- \(D_Y'\subseteq D_Y\) is the target distinction family being tested;
- \(\operatorname{Test}_b\) is the declared adequacy test;
- \(\operatorname{Out}_b\) is the branch outcome.

The branch is evaluated by checking:

\[
D_Y'\subseteq\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_b}(D_X).
\]

---

## 23.2 Branch outcomes

The outcome \(\operatorname{Out}_b\) may be one of the following:

1. **success**: the candidate bridge recovers the target distinctions;
2. **partial success**: the candidate bridge recovers a proper subfamily of the target distinctions;
3. **failure**: the candidate bridge recovers none of the target distinctions or collapses the decisive distinction;
4. **wrong-direction relation**: the candidate bridge shows that the proposed arrow should be reversed, weakened, or reclassified;
5. **ill-typed comparison**: the source and target value spaces or distinction types are not comparable without additional comparison data;
6. **underdeclared branch**: the candidate bridge lacks enough data to run the adequacy test.

These outcomes are not psychological judgements. They are bridge-calibration statuses.

---

## 23.3 Bridge-completion trace

A **bridge-completion trace** is a finite rooted branch tree

\[
\mathcal T_{X\to Y}
\]

whose root is the transfer claim \(X\Rightarrow Y\), whose nodes are bridge-completion branches, and whose edges are repair or refinement steps.

An edge

\[
b_i\to b_j
\]

means that \(b_j\) is a candidate repair, strengthening, weakening, or retyping of \(b_i\) based on the diagnostic output of \(b_i\).

---

## 23.4 Diagnostic failure theorem

### Theorem 23.1 — Failed branches are constructive when they isolate a missing bridge component

Let \(b=(\mathfrak B_b,D_Y',\operatorname{Test}_b,\operatorname{Out}_b)\) be a bridge-completion branch. Suppose \(b\) fails because a specific component \(c\) is absent from \(\mathfrak B_b\), and suppose that every adequate bridge for \(D_Y'\) in the declared bridge class must include a component of type \(c\). Then the failure of \(b\) is constructive: any successful repair inside the declared bridge class must add, replace, or simulate \(c\).

### Proof

Because \(b\) fails, \(D_Y'\not\subseteq\operatorname{Rec}_{\mathfrak B_b}(D_X)\). By hypothesis, the failure is traced to the absence of component \(c\), and every adequate bridge in the declared class requires a component of type \(c\). Therefore any repaired bridge \(\mathfrak B'\) that is adequate for \(D_Y'\) must contain, replace, or simulate \(c\). Thus the failure identifies a necessary repair direction. \(\square\)

This theorem is distinct from No-Free-Transfer. No-Free-Transfer says that an inadequate bridge cannot establish the target. The present theorem says when the failure of a particular inadequate bridge is diagnostically constructive.

for the declared objecthood target.

for every candidate \(O\), then no compatible object family exists.

\boxed{
\text{failure}

Diagnostic:

\boxed{

}
\]

This trace shows that local compatibility is not global objecthood; the missing relation is the gluing/transport relation.

## 23.9 Branch trace lesson

A bridge-completion trace does not merely say:

\[
\text{success}
\quad\text{or}\quad
\text{failure}.
\]

It says:

\[
\boxed{
\text{success for which target distinctions, under which bridge, with which remaining failures?}
}
\]

Thus the trace prevents all-or-nothing verdicts. A branch can fail for Lorentzian scale while succeeding for causal order. A branch can succeed for learning pressure while failing for physical dynamics. A branch can make objecthood well-typed while failing by holonomy.

That is the constructive content of the No-Undeclared-Relation principle.

# 26. Summary

The master principle is:

\[
\boxed{
\text{No declared relation, no legitimate relational inference.}
}
\]

The No-Free-Transfer theorem is the cross-context corollary:

\[
\boxed{
\text{No nontrivial target distinction transfers across closure contexts without encoding or bridge declaration.}
}
\]

The constructive bridge audit method asks:

\[
\boxed{
\text{What bridge preserves the target distinctions?}
}
\]

Equivalently, every worked example asks how the underdeclared dyad \(X\to Y\) becomes the declared triad \(X-\mathfrak B_{X\to Y}-Y\).

If the bridge is missing, one can classify the missing bridge type and search for solved bridge templates in mature theories. A solved template does not automatically transfer. Branch-trace analysis then records which candidate bridges succeed, fail, or partially succeed, and what each result teaches. It becomes a candidate bridge completion that must be calibrated against the target distinctions of the new context.

Thus the no-go is also a method:

\[
\boxed{
\text{find the missing bridge type, then look for a solved bridge of that type.}
}
\]

If the template succeeds, the underdeclared claim becomes inspectable. If it fails, the failure identifies which target distinctions remain unpreserved.

In this sense, both successful and failed bridge completions are learning.
